Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing work isn’t “one-size-fits-all,” but there are key rules you can verify before you sign. Contractors installing or repairing roofs generally must register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the Pennsylvania Attorney General’s office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before work begins, ask for the HIC number and verify it online.

Permits are handled at the municipal level. In many York County municipalities, full roof replacements require a building permit, while repairs often do not. Some boroughs may exempt like-for-like shingle replacement, but you should still confirm with your local code office. In Spring Garden, assume a full replacement will likely require a permit unless your contractor documents otherwise.

Ice-and-water protection is especially important in PA. Residential code practices based on the IRC typically require ice-barrier protection at eaves where ice dams form. Ask your contractor to confirm the required minimum installation height and show how it will be integrated with drip edge and flashing.

For storm claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written, detailed scope of loss from your contractor to submit with your insurance claim. Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—attempts to do so can be insurance fraud. Also verify workers’ compensation coverage through WCAIS (Pennsylvania workers’ comp database) and require proof of insurance before anyone steps onto your roof.

To verify a contractor step-by-step in Spring Garden: (1) confirm their HIC registration online, (2) review the certificate of insurance for general liability naming you as additional insured if applicable, (3) check workers’ comp coverage on WCAIS, and (4) confirm whether your specific roof scope needs a permit with your municipal code office.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Spring Garden

Choosing the right roofer in Spring Garden is mostly about verification and clarity. First, confirm licensing and credentials. In Pennsylvania, contractors should be registered as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the Attorney General’s HIC registry; you can check hic.attorneygeneral.gov and match the company name to the estimate. If the contractor advertises specialized systems (like metal or flat TPO/EPDM), ask for proof of installer certifications from the manufacturer and document it in writing.

Second, get 2–3 itemized quotes. You want line items that separate labor and materials, not a single lump sum. Ask how many layers will be removed, whether decking repairs are included or “allowanced,” what underlayment and ice-and-water shield will be used, and what flashing details are included (chimneys, valleys, vents, and wall transitions). Also ask if the quote includes storm documentation support for insurance claims (photos, measurements, and a written scope of loss).

Third, read the scope for exclusions—especially debris hauling, dumpster fees, yard protection, and warranty terms. A workmanship warranty should be spelled out (length and what it covers), and the materials warranty should be provided by the manufacturer. For payment, don’t pay more than 10–15% upfront, and set a holdback to be released after completion and final inspection. Finally, get a written arrival estimate and completion window. In York County, spring and fall schedules can stretch 4–8 weeks, so you need dates, not just “soon.”
