Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors are required to be registered as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign anything in Carbondale or anywhere in Lackawanna County, verify the HIC number—this is one of the fastest ways to confirm you’re working with a legitimate business. For insurance-related and complex roof work, that verification becomes even more important because you’ll want proper documentation and accountability.

Building permits for roofing vary by municipality. In general, most townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not typical repairs). Some boroughs may exempt like-for-like shingle replacements, but you should confirm with the local code office before work starts—especially when the scope includes structural work, deck replacement, or changes to the roof system.

Ice and water shield is a consistent requirement across Pennsylvania. Residential code (based on IRC) calls for ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas with a history of ice dam formation—conditions that apply to virtually all of Pennsylvania. Ask your contractor to confirm the required minimum installation height and how it will be detailed around valleys, penetrations, and transitions.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from your contractor to submit with your insurance claim. Pennsylvania also prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—it’s considered insurance fraud. Finally, verify workers’ comp coverage using WCAIS (PA’s workers’ compensation database) and require a current certificate of insurance. If you’re unsure where to look, start with: (1) HIC registry online, (2) contractor’s certificate of insurance, and (3) WCAIS confirmation for workers’ comp before anyone climbs on your roof.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Carbondale

Choosing the right roofer in Carbondale starts with verification. First, confirm the contractor is listed as a Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Next, if the contractor advertises IICRC-style certifications for water intrusion or related roofing services, ask for proof and check it through the certifying organization’s registry where applicable. Finally, verify workers’ comp coverage through PA’s WCAIS system and require a certificate of insurance before work begins—no exceptions. Those credential checks protect you if something goes wrong during tear-off, deck repairs, or flashing upgrades.

Then get 2–3 itemized written quotes. The best quotes break down labor and materials clearly (tear-off, disposal, underlayment/ice-and-water shield, flashing, ventilation fixes, decking repairs, and final installation). Avoid “lump sum only” pricing with vague wording. Read the scope for what’s excluded (for example: attic ventilation changes, chimney flashing rebuilds, gutter replacement, or decking repairs beyond a set allowance). If this is an insurance claim, ask whether they include the written scope of loss documentation you’ll need to submit. Make sure warranty details are spelled out: workmanship warranty length and coverage, plus the manufacturer warranty for shingles/underlayment/metal parts or flat membrane.

Payment schedule matters: never pay more than about 10–15% upfront, and use a holdback until completion and cleanup are verified. Ask for an arrival estimate and a completion window in writing so you’re not left waiting for weather or material delays.

What is the best roofing material for Carbondale's climate?
