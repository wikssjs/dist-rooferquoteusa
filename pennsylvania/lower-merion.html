Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors typically must register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign, verify your roofer’s HIC number and make sure the contract matches the business information on file. This is one of the simplest steps homeowners in Lower Merion can take to reduce risk.

Permits also vary by municipality. In most Montgomery County townships, a building permit is generally required for full roof replacements (not routine repairs). Some areas may exempt “like-for-like” shingle swaps, but you should confirm with the local code office before any work begins—especially if you’re changing roof components like ventilation, underlayment, or flashing.

Ice and water shield is not optional in Pennsylvania. Residential code (based on the IRC) requires ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas with a history of ice dam formation—which applies across Pennsylvania. Ask your contractor to confirm the required installation height and details in writing.

For storm damage claims: document everything before cleanup (photos of missing shingles, displaced flashing, interior staining, and ceiling/wall leaks). Request a written scope of loss from the contractor for your insurance paperwork. Pennsylvania law also prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—attempts to “work around” it can create insurance fraud issues.

Finally, verify workers’ comp coverage via WCAIS (Pennsylvania’s workers’ compensation database). A properly covered crew protects you from liability exposure if someone is injured on your property.

Step-by-step for Lower Merion homeowners:

  • Check the contractor’s HIC registration at hic.attorneygeneral.gov and write down the HIC number.
  • Ask for a current certificate of insurance (general liability) naming you as the certificate holder when appropriate.
  • Verify workers’ compensation through WCAIS and confirm the crew is covered for work at your address.
  • Ask the roofer whether a permit is required for a full replacement and who will submit it.
  • Confirm the plan for code-required items (like ice and water shield) on the proposal before scheduling.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Lower Merion roof?

With 81.7% of Lower Merion’s housing stock built before 1980, many homeowners are dealing with aging roofs that are at or past their typical service life. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle adds pressure—ice dam conditions can drive moisture higher under shingles, and once water reaches the deck, damage can spread even if the leak “seems” small.

In practice, I advise owners to think beyond the visible spot. If the problem is limited to one area and the roof still has remaining lifespan, a targeted repair can be cost-effective. But if you’re seeing multiple leak points over a few seasons, patchwork often costs more in the long run because each repair carries labor, materials, and the risk that surrounding components are already compromised. Similarly, older roofs in Lower Merion often require careful attention to ice-and-water shield coverage at eaves and valleys—if those layers are missing or deteriorated, repairs tend to become temporary.

One example: if your 18–20-year-old architectural shingle roof has two active leak areas, a “fix every leak” approach can land near the higher end of repair-style spending and still leave you with an aging system. In that scenario, replacement in the range of $24,000–$45,000 is often justified because you reset the roof system, improve underlayment and ventilation, and reduce repeat claims.

Frequently asked questions — roofing in Lower Merion

Can I put new shingles over old shingles in Pennsylvania?

Sometimes, but it’s not automatically the right move in Lower Merion. In many Pennsylvania homes—especially those built before 1980—existing asphalt layers can already be multiple, and adding another layer can hide problems like a deteriorating roof deck, poor ventilation, or failing underlayment. That matters because Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycles and ice dam risk can drive moisture under older shingles and into the deck, so “covering” it may delay visible issues while the underlying system keeps aging. Even when an overlay is permitted, you still need a proper fastening plan, correct flashing details, and code-required ice-and-water shield at eaves.

If your roof has two or more shingle layers, many homeowners end up better off with a full tear-off—especially when quote pricing in the $9,500–$24,000 band for asphalt replacement is close to overlay costs once labor, underlayment, and decking corrections are included. A site inspection is the only reliable way to know whether a clean, code-compliant overlay is feasible.

What causes ice dams on roofs in Lower Merion?

Ice dams form when heat escapes into the attic/roof system and melts snow near the warm areas of your roof. The meltwater then refreezes at the colder eaves, creating a ridge of ice that blocks drainage and can push water under shingles. In Lower Merion, this risk is heightened on older roofs because insulation levels and ventilation paths were often less effective than today’s standards. Roof geometry matters too—valleys, north-facing slopes, and complex multi-facet designs can collect snow and stay colder longer. If your eaves aren’t protected with properly installed ice-and-water shield (ice barrier), water can migrate into vulnerable edges.

Another common contributor is air leakage into the attic combined with inadequate soffit intake. During replacement, contractors often recommend upgraded underlayment and ventilation to reduce melt/refreeze cycles. That’s one reason a full replacement quote in the architectural shingle range (commonly $9,500–$45,000) can be higher than a simple repair—because it’s addressing the root cause, not just stopping a current leak.

What should a roofing quote include in Lower Merion?

A strong roofing quote for Lower Merion should read like a construction plan, not an estimate guess. Make sure it includes: the material type and grade (for example, architectural shingles versus impact-resistant options), the tear-off scope (including how many layers will be removed), underlayment details, and whether ice-and-water shield will be installed at eaves and any other required locations. Include flashing scope (valleys, chimneys, penetrations), ventilation changes, and line items for decking repairs if discovered during tear-off. If your contractor is also replacing gutters, the quote should separate gutter replacement and accessories like guards.

Also confirm warranty language: workmanship warranty length and manufacturer system coverage. For storm work or insurance claims, the proposal should align to a written scope of loss and include documentation support. If you only receive a lump sum with no breakdown, it’s harder to compare bids fairly or understand what’s excluded.

How do I know if my Lower Merion roof needs replacement?
