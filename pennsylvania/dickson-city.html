Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors are required to register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office (hic.attorneygeneral.gov). Before you sign anything, verify the contractor’s HIC number matches the company you’re hiring. If a roofer won’t provide it, or insists you “sign first,” that’s a serious warning.

Building permits vary by municipality. Most townships typically require a building permit for full roof replacement (not repairs). Some boroughs may exempt like-for-like shingle replacement, but you still need to confirm with the local code office before work starts—especially if you’re changing roof systems, doing structural deck repairs, replacing chimneys, or altering ventilation pathways.

Ice-and-water protection is also a key regulatory and safety topic. Pennsylvania residential code (based on IRC) requires ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas with ice dam history—which applies across virtually all of Pennsylvania. Ask your contractor to confirm the required minimum installation height and where it will be applied on your specific roof edges.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from the contractor to support your insurance submission. Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—attempting to “skip your deductible” is considered insurance fraud.

Finally, confirm workers’ compensation coverage by checking the WCAIS database (PA workers’ comp). A contractor without WC creates liability exposure for the homeowner.

Step-by-step for Dickson City: (1) Check the HIC registration at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. (2) Request a certificate of insurance (general liability) and verify dates and coverage limits. (3) Provide the contractor’s WC info and confirm via WCAIS that the crew is covered. (4) Ask whether a permit will be needed for a full reroof or deck repairs, then confirm with the borough/township code office.

What causes ice dams on roofs in Dickson City?

Ice dams happen when snow on your roof melts and then refreezes near the eaves, creating a blockage that can force water under shingles. In Dickson City and across Pennsylvania, the freeze-thaw cycle is a major contributor, particularly when warm air escapes into the attic and warms the roof surface above the cold eaves. Older homes—especially those built before modern insulation and ventilation standards—often have temperature imbalances that make ice dam conditions more likely.

Common causes include inadequate attic insulation, blocked soffit intake vents, weak or incorrectly sized ridge ventilation, and older roof edge details that aren’t protected with ice-and-water underlayment. Gaps around penetrations, failing valley flashing, and poor gutter drainage can also increase meltwater pathways toward the eaves. Once ice forms, wind and repeated refreeze cycles can drive water entry and speed up shingle deterioration.

That’s why reputable contractors typically recommend ice-and-water shield at eaves and critical roof edges, along with ventilation improvements, not just “fixing the leak.” If your roof is older (and many are), budgeting for underlayment and edge detailing is part of responsible roofing—especially since interior leak damage can cost far more than the preventative materials that protect against meltwater refreeze.
