Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors must be registered as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign anything for a roof replacement in Allegheny or anywhere in Westmoreland County, verify the contractor’s HIC number matches the company name and address you’re hiring.

Building permits vary by municipality. Many townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not just repairs). Some boroughs may exempt like-for-like shingle replacement, but you must confirm with your local code office before work begins—don’t rely on word-of-mouth.

Ice and water shield is a big compliance point. Pennsylvania residential code, based on the IRC, requires ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas with ice-dam history—which applies broadly across Pennsylvania. Ask your contractor to state the required minimum installation height (and where it applies on your roof: eaves, valleys, and roof-to-wall transitions).

For storm claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from your roofer to submit with your insurance claim. Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible, which is considered insurance fraud.

Step-by-step verification for Allegheny homeowners: (1) check the HIC registration online; (2) request a current certificate of insurance (general liability) naming you as additional insured if applicable; (3) verify workers’ compensation coverage via PA’s WCAIS workers’ comp database; and (4) keep copies of both certificates and the signed contract on file.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Allegheny

Choosing the right roofer in Allegheny starts with verifying credentials before you ever discuss payment. First, confirm IICRC certification (where applicable) and Pennsylvania licensing information. For PA contractor status, check required registrations through the official online resources, and for workers’ comp and contract-related documentation, request current proof directly from the company.

Next, get 2–3 written quotes that break down labor and materials rather than one lump sum. A quality quote should list tear-off scope, underlayment/ice barrier coverage, decking replacement allowances, flashing and ventilation details, and disposal. Make sure the quote clearly states what is included and what is excluded, including whether insurance claim documentation, photos, and a written scope of loss are part of the service.

Warranty matters in western Pennsylvania winters. Ask for the length of the workmanship warranty and the manufacturer warranty for materials. Also confirm the warranty applies to properly installed ice barrier and ventilation—these details drive performance in Allegheny’s freeze-thaw season.

For payment scheduling, avoid large upfront deposits. A safe rule is: never pay more than 10–15% upfront, and request a holdback until the job is complete and clean. Finally, get a written arrival estimate and completion window.
