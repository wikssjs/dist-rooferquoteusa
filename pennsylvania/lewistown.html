Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors are typically required to register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the Pennsylvania Attorney General’s Office. Before you sign anything, verify the contractor’s HIC number at hic.attorneygeneral.gov (and confirm the business name matches the proposal and contract). If a contractor won’t provide their HIC number or treats it like “too much trouble,” that’s a red flag.

Building permits for roofing vary by municipality. Most townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not just repairs). Some boroughs may exempt like-for-like shingle replacements, but the only safe approach is to confirm with your local code office before work starts. For Lewistown homeowners, the permit requirement often depends on whether you’re doing a full replacement versus isolated patching.

Ice-and-water shield is also a compliance issue: PA residential code (based on the IRC) requires ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas with a history of ice dam formation—conditions that apply across Pennsylvania. Ask your roofer to confirm the required minimum installation height and where it will be applied at your specific roof edges and valleys.

Storm insurance claims: document everything before cleanup, and request a written scope of loss from the contractor to submit with your claim. Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—doing so can create insurance fraud risk. Also verify workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database) and request proof of coverage and a current certificate of insurance before work begins. A reputable contractor will provide these quickly.

Step-by-step for Lewistown: (1) check HIC registration online, (2) verify workers’ comp coverage via WCAIS, (3) ask for COI showing general liability and workers’ comp endorsements, (4) confirm permit needs with your borough/township code office, and (5) review the written scope before any deposit.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Lewistown roof?

With 92% of Lewistown’s housing stock built before 1980, many roofs are approaching—or already past—their expected service life. In Mifflin County, the repair vs. replace decision typically hinges on how old the roof is, whether the damage is isolated or widespread, and whether the decking remains solid. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle and winter ice dam risk can accelerate shingle deterioration, especially along eaves and valleys on roofs under roughly 3/12 pitch—water backs up, underlayment gets saturated, and repairs can temporarily patch symptoms while the underlying materials continue to fail.

Practically speaking, older homes in Lewistown often require more than a “swap the shingles” approach because the deck and underlayment are the hidden variables. If you have a small active leak but the roof is still young, repair is usually the smartest move. If you’re dealing with multiple leak points (around dormers, chimney flashing, or repeated ice-dam patterns) or evidence of soft decking, replacement becomes more cost-effective because patchwork over 3–5 years tends to cost more in labor and frequent return visits.

Example: one homeowner may spend within the $350–$2,200 repair band for a localized leak around a boot flashing—if the roof deck is dry and shingles are otherwise in good shape. But if the same roof has two layers, failing underlayment, and rotted decking discovered during tear-off, a full asphalt replacement often lands closer to $19,000–$34,000, which is justified because it resets the system: deck, underlayment, ice barrier, ventilation, and flashing.

Bottom line: we recommend repair when we can isolate the failure and keep the rest of the roofing system dry and intact. We recommend replacement when the roof is past its lifespan or when the damage shows the system has already been compromised.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Lewistown

Choosing the right roofing contractor in Lewistown comes down to verification, clarity, and proof—not just a low price. Start with licensing and certification. In Pennsylvania, confirm the contractor is registered as a Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) at the Attorney General’s HIC lookup (hic.attorneygeneral.gov). For workmanship credentials, ask whether the company holds IICRC-style certification relevant to roofing/repairs (if provided by your contractor), and verify any professional status through the certifying organization’s public directory.

Then request 2–3 itemized written quotes. A strong quote should break down labor and materials (tear-off, decking repair allowance, underlayment/ice-and-water shield, drip edge, flashing, ventilation, shingle/metal/roof membrane, and disposal). Avoid lump-sum offers that don’t explain what’s included. Pay attention to the scope: what is excluded (existing vent removal, chimney work, permit fees, gutter replacement, deck replacement allowances), and whether insurance documentation or photos are included for storm claims.

Warranties matter. Ask for (1) a workmanship warranty (and length), and (2) the manufacturer warranty details for materials like shingles or metal panels—equipment and installation method can affect warranty validity. For payment schedule, never pay more than 10–15% upfront; hold back until the job is complete and final inspection is done. Also get the arrival estimate and completion window in writing, including weather contingencies common to Mifflin County.

How do I know if my Lewistown roof needs replacement?

In Lewistown, the most reliable way to know if replacement is needed is to combine your roof’s age with inspection findings from the surface and the attic. If your roof is past the expected lifespan—especially in homes built before 1980, where materials and installation practices were different—you may see signs like frequent leaks, missing granules, curled shingles, or recurring ice-dam damage near eaves and valleys. If you’re unsure, ask the contractor to inspect the attic for water staining, check ventilation, and evaluate whether the underlayment is saturated or failing. What is the best roofing material for Lewistown's climate?
