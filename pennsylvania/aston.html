Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing work is regulated in important ways that homeowners in Aston should take seriously before signing. First, Pennsylvania requires roofing contractors to register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Always verify a contractor’s HIC number before work begins and before you provide any deposit.

Building permits for roofing vary by municipality. Most townships in Delaware County require a building permit for full roof replacements (not repairs). Some boroughs may exempt true like-for-like shingle swaps, but you should confirm with your Aston-area building department or code office before the contractor mobilizes—especially in older neighborhoods where tear-off and code upgrades are more likely.

Ice and water shield is another regulation issue. PA residential code (based on IRC principles) requires ice-barrier protection at the eaves in areas with history of ice dam formation, which applies broadly across Pennsylvania. Ask your contractor to confirm the required minimum installation height and how they’ll integrate the membrane at valleys, transitions, and roof penetrations.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup: photos of interior damage (ceilings/walls), exterior failure points, and a written scope of work. PA law also prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—this is considered insurance fraud. Finally, verify workers’ comp coverage via WCAIS (PA’s workers’ compensation database) and request proof of coverage. Uninsured roofers create liability exposure for homeowners.

Step-by-step for Aston homeowners: (1) confirm HIC registration online; (2) ask for a current certificate of insurance for general liability; (3) verify workers’ comp on WCAIS and ensure the crew matches the coverage; (4) ask which permit applies and who files it; (5) get the scope and documentation in writing for insurance and inspection needs.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Aston roof?

With 68.9% of Aston’s housing stock built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), many homeowners are dealing with roofs that are at or near the end of their expected service life. In Delaware County, the repair-versus-replace decision usually comes down to three things: the age of the roof, whether the damage is isolated or spreading, and whether the decking and underlayment are still sound. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle, including ice dam risk that becomes common on lower-slope areas and roofs under about 3/12 pitch, accelerates deterioration along eaves and valleys—so “it only leaks a little” can still be the early sign of edge failure.

In practical terms, a smaller leak repair is often justified when shingles are relatively new and the decking is healthy. But when multiple leak points appear, the underlayment and ice barrier are often compromised beyond one spot. If your deck is softened, you’ll pay for repeated repairs, and you also risk hidden moisture that can lead to interior drywall damage.

Here’s a real budgeting example: if you’re seeing isolated leaks on a roof under 15 years old, repair might land in the $8,500–$16,000 range depending on access and how much needs to be opened up. If that same home is closer to 15–25 years old and you have several leak areas—or signs of repeated ice dam staining—patchwork can cost more over time, and replacement often fits better within the $16,500–$32,000 band.

Because many Aston homes are older and built on roofs exposed to seasonal temperature swings, I recommend treating repair as a smart option only when the roof deck and the roof’s underlayment system are still in good shape. Otherwise, replacement can be the cost-effective move that reduces the chance of recurring leaks.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Aston

Choosing a roofing contractor in Aston starts with credential checks that protect you long before the first shingle gets nailed down. For liability and risk, obtain a current certificate of insurance for general liability and confirm workers’ comp coverage through WCAIS. Ask that the proof includes the actual business name and that the crew performing the work is covered.

Next, request 2–3 itemized written quotes. You want line items that show labor, tear-off, disposal, underlayment/ice barrier, ventilation components, decking replacement, flashing, and gutters if included—rather than a single lump-sum number. Make sure the scope clearly states what’s excluded (for example, hidden deck repair allowance, structural repairs, or permit filing responsibilities) and whether insurance claim documentation is included for storm jobs.

Warranty matters in two layers: workmanship and materials. Ask for the workmanship warranty length, and ask for the equipment and materials warranty details (including who supplies it and what voids coverage). Payment should be controlled—never pay more than about 10–15% upfront. Hold back funds until the job is complete, cleaned up, and the final walkthrough is done. Finally, timeline expectations should be written: when the crew will arrive and the estimated completion window, because Pennsylvania scheduling during peak season can shift due to labor demand across Delaware County.

What causes ice dams on roofs in Aston?

Ice dams form when heat from the home melts snow on the roof surface, and that meltwater refreezes near the colder eaves, causing a backup of water under shingles and into vulnerable roof edges. In Aston and across Pennsylvania, the freeze-thaw cycle makes this pattern common, especially on older roofs where insulation and ventilation may not be ideal. Common contributors include poor attic ventilation (trapping warm air), insufficient insulation (allowing roof deck warming), air leaks that push warm air into the attic, and roof geometry where ice accumulates at valleys or overhangs. Older housing stock—about 68.9% built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024)—often means systems weren’t installed with today’s ventilation standards in mind, which can raise the odds of eave and valley leaks. Another factor is whether the required ice-and-water protection (ice barrier) is installed correctly at the eaves. A reliable solution typically combines proper ice barrier installation with ventilation and insulation improvements, not just adding a few shingles. If you’re seeing edge leaks, dark staining at the gutters, or roofline icicles paired with interior moisture, it’s time for a leak diagnosis and a repair or replacement plan.
