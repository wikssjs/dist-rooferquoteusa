Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, start by verifying that your roofing contractor is properly registered. Contractors who do home improvement work must be listed as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign a contract, check for the contractor’s HIC number and confirm it matches the entity on the proposal.

Permits for roofing also vary by municipality. In most Chester County townships and similar PA jurisdictions, a building permit is typically required for full roof replacements (not minor repairs). Some boroughs may allow certain like-for-like shingle swaps without a permit, but you should confirm with East Brandywine’s local code office before work begins—especially if your project includes structural changes, re-decking, new skylights/vents, or any changes to roof framing.

Ice and water shield requirements are important statewide. Based on the residential code (based on IRC), Pennsylvania requires ice-barrier protection at roof eaves in areas prone to ice dams—which applies broadly across the Commonwealth. Ask your contractor to confirm the required installation height and whether the ice-and-water membrane will be properly extended at valleys and problem transitions.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from the contractor so it can support your insurance claim, and understand that PA law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—doing so is treated as insurance fraud. Finally, verify workers’ compensation coverage through WCAIS (PA’s workers’ comp database) and ask for proof of coverage; uninsured roofers increase liability exposure for the homeowner.

Step-by-step for East Brandywine homeowners: (1) verify HIC registration online; (2) ask for a current certificate of insurance showing general liability and, if applicable, workers’ comp; (3) check proof of workers’ comp in WCAIS; (4) confirm whether your municipality requires a permit for a full replacement and make sure it’s included in the contractor’s schedule.

Then get 2–3 itemized, written quotes that break out labor and materials separately (tear-off, disposal, decking, underlayment, flashing, ventilation upgrades, and trim), rather than one lump sum. Can I put new shingles over old shingles in Pennsylvania?

Sometimes, but it’s often not the best choice—especially for older Chester County roofs. In Pennsylvania, homeowners will ask about “overlay” installs to save tear-off costs; however, layering can trap moisture and limit your ability to evaluate and correct decking and underlayment condition. East Brandywine has a large share of homes built before 1980 (38.5%), and those roofs frequently have multiple existing layers or ventilation/ice-barrier details that need correction. If there are existing leaks, curling shingles, soft decking, or damaged flashing, overlay is usually a red flag because the new shingles won’t fix the underlying failure point. Overlays also complicate eave and flashing transitions, which can affect performance during winter freeze-thaw cycles.

From a cost standpoint, overlay may appear cheaper upfront, but once you’re dealing with decking issues or ice-dam related underlayment problems, replacement often becomes the responsible approach within the typical architectural shingle band of $8,000–$35,000.

What causes ice dams on roofs in East Brandywine?

Ice dams form when roof heat melts snow, and the meltwater refreezes at colder areas near the eaves or gutters, blocking proper drainage. In East Brandywine, ice dam risk is influenced by winter temperature swings and how your attic is performing. Common causes include warm air escaping into the attic, inadequate insulation, air leaks at the attic floor, and ventilation issues that don’t keep the roof deck evenly cold. On older roof lines, the ice-and-water shield may be missing, worn, or improperly detailed, which is why many replacements in Pennsylvania require upgrading this protection at eaves. Homes with multiple layers and aging underlayment can also make it harder to identify the true source of moisture until the tear-off reveals the problem.

The best prevention approach is a combination: correct attic insulation and air sealing, proper soffit and ridge ventilation, and correct installation of ice-and-water barrier at the eaves. If you’re seeing repeated icicles or interior ceiling staining, it’s a strong signal that the underlying system needs attention—not just a quick chip-and-clean.
