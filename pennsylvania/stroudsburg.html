Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, reputable roofing contractors should be properly registered and insured before they start work. Roofing contractors must register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign anything, verify the company’s HIC number matches the contractor who will perform the work.

Permits for roofing also vary by municipality. Most townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not repairs). Some areas may exempt like-for-like shingle replacements, but you should confirm with the local code office before work begins—because scheduling inspections can affect your timeline.

On code-protection items, Pennsylvania residential roofing generally requires ice-barrier protection at the eaves (based on IRC), and this is particularly important for Stroudsburg roofs because winter ice dams are a recurring issue in the region. Ask your contractor to state the required minimum installation height and show it on the proposal.

For storm insurance claims: document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from the contractor to support your claim. Be aware that Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—attempts to do so can cross into insurance fraud.

Also verify workers’ compensation. You can check via WCAIS (Pennsylvania workers’ comp database) that the crew is covered. Uninsured roofers create liability exposure for homeowners.

Step-by-step in Stroudsburg: (1) Check HIC registration at the Attorney General site; (2) ask for a Certificate of Insurance showing general liability and workers’ comp; (3) verify workers’ comp coverage in WCAIS; (4) confirm whether your Stroudsburg township requires a permit for a full replacement; (5) require the permit plan or at least a written statement of responsibility for permits before start date.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Stroudsburg

Choosing a contractor in Stroudsburg starts with verification. First, confirm the company’s Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Next, ask about certification and insurance documentation. If you’re considering re-roofing details like ice-barrier and ventilation upgrades, insist the crew can demonstrate prior installation work that matches Pennsylvania requirements (especially ice protection at eaves).

Then get 2–3 itemized written quotes. “Itemized” means a breakdown that separates labor from materials and specifies scope items such as tear-off, disposal, underlayment, starter strips, flashing, ice-and-water placement, ridge/venting components, and decking repairs. Avoid lump-sum proposals that don’t explain what’s included or excluded—this is where misunderstandings happen.

Read the scope line by line. Make sure it addresses whether insurance documentation is supported (photos, measurements, and a written scope of loss if you’re filing a storm claim). Confirm workmanship warranty length and what it covers—then review material warranties separately (shingles, metal components, or TPO/EPDM membrane). For payment scheduling, never pay more than 10–15% upfront; hold back until key milestones are complete and the job is cleaned and finalized.

Finally, request a written arrival estimate and completion window. During peak spring–fall scheduling in Monroe County, timing can affect how long tarps sit if storms move through, so your plan should reflect realistic weather windows.
