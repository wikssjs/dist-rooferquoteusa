Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, reputable roofing contractors should be registered as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign in Dunbar (or anywhere in Fayette County), verify the contractor’s HIC number—don’t rely on a business card or verbal assurance.

Permits vary by municipality. For roofing work, most townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not typical repairs). Some boroughs may exempt like-for-like shingle replacements, but you should still confirm with your local code office before work starts. If structural decking repairs or changes to the roof system are involved, permits are much more likely.

Ice and water shield rules matter in nearly all of Pennsylvania because ice dam formation is common. Ask your contractor to confirm the required installation height at eaves and to specify how they’ll install protection around penetrations like chimneys and vents.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss you can submit to your carrier. Also note: Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—claims tied to roof work can be complicated, and you should protect yourself.

Workers’ comp is non-negotiable. Verify coverage through WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database). If the crew is uninsured, you can be exposed to liability. In Dunbar, your best step-by-step check is: (1) confirm HIC registration online, (2) request a certificate of insurance (general liability + workers’ comp) and confirm dates/matching company names, (3) check WCAIS proof of workers’ compensation coverage, and (4) ask your contractor to state whether a permit is required for your specific scope.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Dunbar roof?

With 67% of Dunbar’s housing stock built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), many homeowners are dealing with roofs that are at or beyond expected lifespan. The repair-vs-replace decision in Fayette County comes down to roof age, the extent of leaks, and—most importantly—whether the decking and underlayment are still intact. A repair that seems “small” from the attic can become expensive once we remove shingles and find widespread water intrusion.

Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle also changes the math. Ice dams and winter moisture tend to hit the eaves and low spots first, and in many cases those areas deteriorate faster than the center of the roof. If your home’s roof has a pitch under about 3/12 or has roof-edge details that allow backup water, leaks can creep along the underside of shingles even when the visible damage looks limited. On older roofs, the deck is often the limiting factor.

Here’s how it typically plays out locally: if your roof is under 15 years old and you have an isolated leak (for example, a failed flashing around a plumbing vent), repair is often justified because the shingles still have useful life left. But if you’re seeing multiple leak areas across seasons, or if patchwork has been attempted before, replacement is usually the cost-effective move over the next several years. If the roof is 25+ years old, any sign of soft decking, widespread underlayment failure, or recurring leaks means replacement should be prioritized—especially to stop the interior damage before winter.

To put dollar logic on it: a homeowner might start with a localized fix thinking it’s within the typical repair band (for small leak/isolated damage). But if the deck is compromised, that “cheap” attempt can lead to a bigger bill. In many Dunbar cases, it’s smarter to invest in a full replacement in the $16,000–$18,000 to $19,000–$38,000 ranges depending on materials and complexity, rather than paying multiple service calls.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Dunbar

Choosing the right roofer in Dunbar means verifying credentials the same day you’re shopping, not after you sign. Start with PA licensing/registration: confirm the contractor’s Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration through the Attorney General’s HIC registry (hic.attorneygeneral.gov). Then ask for proof of certification/credentials tied to roofing work (for example, certifications for installation systems when applicable) and verify the company’s identity matches the insurance paperwork.

Next, require 2–3 itemized written quotes. You want a breakdown by labor and material, including tear-off scope, underlayment/ice-and-water details, decking replacement allowance (if needed), flashing, venting, and the warranty terms. A lump-sum price is harder to compare and easier to dispute if conditions change.

Read the scope like a checklist: what’s excluded (structural repairs, skylight work, chimney flashing rebuilds, gutter replacement), and what’s included. If you’re doing an insurance claim, ask whether the contractor will provide written storm documentation and a scope you can submit.

Warranty matters in Pennsylvania weather. Ask for (1) a workmanship warranty length for installation and (2) the manufacturer warranty for materials. Also confirm whether they cover what happens at the edges and penetrations—ice-dam failures often start there. Payment should be conservative: never pay more than 10–15% upfront; hold back until the job is complete and debris is cleaned. Finally, insist on a written arrival estimate and an expected completion window; in peak season, reputable crews in the Fayette County area often schedule out 3–6 weeks for asphalt and longer for metal and flat systems.
