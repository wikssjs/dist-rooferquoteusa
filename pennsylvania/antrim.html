What affects the price of roofing in Antrim

If you get two roof bids for the “same” house in Franklin County, it’s common to see differences that land in the 30–50% range. The reason isn’t just contractor preference—it’s how each company measures your roof footprint and how many hidden issues they assume versus uncover. In older Antrim homes, a roof that appears straightforward can involve multiple tear-off layers, repair of plank or spaced-sheathing deck issues, and additional ice-and-water protection at vulnerable roofline areas. Material choice also drives variation: asphalt shingle systems often land in the economy-to-architectural range (for example, asphalt replacement estimates commonly start around $11,000–$21,000 and can move higher for larger roofs), while metal standing seam pricing typically reflects longer-term materials and more detailed flashing work.

Here are the biggest cost drivers we see in Antrim:

  • Roof size and pitch: larger roof areas (“squares”) and steeper pitches increase labor and safety time; accessibility can add cost even when materials are similar.
  • Material type: asphalt shingles are usually the most cost-effective; metal can be a strong longevity move; TPO/EPDM for flat sections requires specialty installation details.
  • Layers to remove: Franklin County’s older housing stock (52.1% built before 1980) frequently means two or three existing layers, increasing tear-off time and disposal.
  • Decking condition: once shingles are removed, we sometimes find soft spots and patchwork needed to return the roof to a sound surface.
  • Freeze-thaw and ice dam risk: repeated winter cycles can worsen compromised eaves/valleys; ice-and-water shield and ventilation choices matter.

Two practical Antrim examples: (1) A small ranch with intact decking and a single layer may stay closer to the lower asphalt replacement band (often $11,000–$21,000), while (2) a 2½-story farmhouse with valleys, dormers, and two existing shingle layers can jump into the higher asphalt or mixed-scope ranges because of tear-off plus decking replacement and underlayment upgrades. For low-slope sections, a membrane replacement can be priced differently than steep roofs due to membrane detailing around penetrations and perimeter conditions.

Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors must be registered as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office (hic.attorneygeneral.gov). Before you sign anything in Antrim, verify the contractor’s HIC number—don’t rely on a phone photo of a card. Building permit requirements also vary by municipality: most townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not repairs), while some boroughs may exempt strictly “like-for-like” shingle replacements. Because Antrim homeowners can be in different township jurisdictions around Franklin County, confirm with your local code office before work starts.

Ice and water shield is a Pennsylvania residential code expectation (based on the IRC) for eaves in areas where ice dams occur, which applies to virtually all of Pennsylvania. Ask your contractor to confirm the minimum installation height they will use at eaves so your system is built for the freeze-thaw reality.

For storm insurance claims, documentation matters. Take photos and request a written scope of work that matches the visible damage so you can include it with your claim. Pennsylvania law also prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible; that’s considered insurance fraud.

Workers’ comp is a safety and liability issue. Verify coverage using WCAIS (Pennsylvania workers’ comp database) that the crew is covered. Uninsured roofers working on your property can expose you to liability.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Antrim roof?

With about 52.1% of homes in the Antrim area built before 1980, many roofs in Franklin County are beyond “new” construction expectations—meaning the decision to repair versus replace depends heavily on what’s happening under the shingles. The repair vs. replace choice usually comes down to the roof age, how many leak locations are present, and whether the decking is still sound after inspection. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle can increase ice-dam risk, especially on roofs that aren’t well protected at the eaves, and that can accelerate shingle deterioration even when only one area appears to be leaking.

In Antrim, we often see three common scenarios. If the roof is under 15 years old with an isolated leak, a repair can make sense because the shingles and underlayment typically still have meaningful remaining service life. If the roof is 15–25 years old and you have multiple leak areas, patching tends to turn into recurring repairs over a few seasons because water finds new weak points at the roofline, penetrations, and valley intersections. For roofs 25+ years old, replacement is usually the best value because the design lifespan has likely passed and the decking and underlayment may already be compromised.

A concrete example: if an active leak has caused a localized failure, a repair might be in the $500–$3,500 range for isolated damage. But if that repair is on a 20+ year roof with multiple failed areas, replacing can land you in a higher band—often $21,000–$38,000 for larger, multi-area situations. In many cases, the “extra” cost of replacement is justified because it stops repeated labor and prevents interior damage that costs far more than the difference between patchwork and a full system.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Antrim

Choosing the right roofer in Antrim is mostly about verification and clarity. First, confirm the contractor is registered as a Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) with the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Next, verify workers’ compensation coverage through WCAIS (Pennsylvania workers’ comp database) so you know the crew is covered. For certification claims like IICRC, look for the contractor’s specific certification listing and confirm it matches the company—not just an individual’s resume. Also ask for a certificate of insurance showing general liability and workers’ comp.

When you request quotes, get 2–3 written, itemized estimates rather than a lump sum. Your quote should break out labor and materials, including tear-off scope, underlayment/ice-and-water shield, flashing details, decking repairs, venting changes (if needed), and disposal. Make sure the scope lists what’s included in leak repair and what’s not—especially if there’s suspected decking moisture or valley/step-flashing replacement.

Warranty matters in Pennsylvania. Ask for the workmanship warranty term and whether there’s a separate materials warranty from the shingle or membrane manufacturer. Ask how long the system is warranted and what conditions void it (for example, improper installation of underlayment or excluded ventilation upgrades).

For payment schedule, never allow more than about 10–15% upfront; hold back until the work is complete and cleaned. Timing should be documented: get an arrival estimate and a completion window in writing.

Can I put new shingles over old shingles in Pennsylvania?

What causes ice dams on roofs in Antrim?

Ice dams typically happen when heat escapes into the attic and melts snow on the roof deck, then that meltwater refreezes at the cold eaves, forming a “dam” that blocks runoff. In Antrim, the freeze-thaw cycle is relentless enough that older homes—particularly those built before 1980—can experience more pronounced ice dam effects when insulation levels are outdated, ventilation is unbalanced, or the eave protection isn’t up to modern expectations. Ice dam risk is also higher on roofs with complicated geometry (valleys, dormers, and roofline transitions) because those areas can hold cold spots and create flow paths that concentrate water.

Pennsylvania code expectations (based on the IRC) include ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas with ice dam history, which applies to virtually all of Pennsylvania. In practical terms, that means proper ice-and-water shield coverage at eaves and correct installation height, plus a ventilation plan that reduces interior heat loss. If your roof is already leaking or you’re noticing staining in the attic near eaves, it’s usually a sign to inspect underlayment and drainage paths, not just patch shingles.

The quote should also list the materials grade and product type—like whether you’re selecting economy 3-tab, architectural shingles, or an impact-resistant option.

For pricing reference, you may see asphalt replacement estimates that start around $11,000–$21,000 and move higher depending on roof size, pitch, and complexity. If a bid doesn’t discuss tear-off and decking assumptions on an older Franklin County roof, it’s missing the details that usually drive the final number.
