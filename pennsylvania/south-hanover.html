Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors are required to register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office (hic.attorneygeneral.gov). Before you sign anything, verify the contractor’s HIC number and that it matches the company you’re hiring. For roofing work, permits also matter: building permit requirements vary by municipality, but most townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (repairs are often treated differently). Some boroughs may exempt like-for-like shingle replacements, yet you should confirm with South Hanover’s local code office before work begins.

One roofing requirement that applies broadly across Pennsylvania is ice and water barrier protection at eaves in areas where ice dams can form—this is typically addressed under residential code based on the IRC. Ask your contractor to confirm the required minimum installation height and where the ice barrier will be applied at valleys, transitions, and penetrations.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup: take wide and close-up photos of damage, and keep copies of any communication. Many homeowners run into issues when the scope of work is unclear, so request a written scope of loss describing what was damaged and what will be replaced. Pennsylvania law also prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible; it’s considered insurance fraud.

Finally, verify workers’ comp coverage via WCAIS (the PA workers’ comp database). If you hire an uninsured crew, you may carry liability exposure on your property. To verify step-by-step: check the contractor’s HIC online first, then request a current certificate of insurance for general liability and workers’ compensation, and confirm the workers’ comp status through WCAIS before any tear-off.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your South Hanover roof?

With roughly 35.5% of South Hanover’s housing stock built before 1980, many homeowners are dealing with roofs that have outlasted their original design expectations—or that have developed weak points from years of winter stress. In Dauphin County, the repair versus replace decision depends on roof age, how widespread the leaks are, and whether the decking is still solid after we remove shingles. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle accelerates deterioration, particularly at eaves and along shallow roof runs where ice dams are more likely; when water backs up under shingles, it can spread damage faster than many owners realize.

On older roofs, we often find multiple shingle layers and compromised flashing at chimneys, valleys, and transitions. If the damage is isolated (for example, a localized leak around a boot, a small section of flashing, or a few missing shingles), repair can make sense. But if there are multiple leak areas across different slopes or repeated failures of underlayment and flashing, patchwork usually costs more over time because the underlying water pathway remains. For example, if you’re choosing between “repair now, replace later” and replacing in one full project, the difference can be justified. A roof repair is commonly in the $750–$4,500 band for truly isolated issues; however, once repairs spread into several sections and require re-flashing, the total often starts approaching replacement territory—where architectural shingle replacements may run within the broader $12,000–$55,000 range depending on size and complexity.

The goal in South Hanover isn’t to sell the most expensive option—it’s to protect the interior and prevent repeat leaks during the next cold season by matching your decision to the condition we uncover during inspection.

How to choose a roofing contractor in South Hanover

Choosing the right roofer in South Hanover means verifying credentials and getting pricing you can actually compare. Start by confirming Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration through the PA Attorney General’s Office (hic.attorneygeneral.gov). For licensing, ask for the contractor’s PA license information where applicable and ensure the company you’re hiring matches the details on those public listings. Then check workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS (the PA workers’ comp database). This is especially important for roofing because liability and jobsite injuries are real risks, and homeowners shouldn’t “learn the hard way” after work begins.

Next, require 2–3 itemized written quotes. A good quote breaks out labor, tear-off/disposal, underlayment and ice barrier, decking repairs, flashing, ventilation work, and material selections—not just a single lump sum. If you’re filing an insurance claim, ask whether the contractor will provide storm documentation you can submit with your insurance packet (photos, measurements, and a written scope of loss). Carefully read what’s excluded: for example, whether skylight flashing, chimney re-flashing, gutter replacement, and ventilation upgrades are included or treated as add-ons.

On warranty and payment terms, insist on a written workmanship warranty (how long they stand behind installation) and confirm separate manufacturer coverage for materials. Payment should follow a responsible schedule—never pay more than 10–15% upfront, and hold back until the job is complete and cleaned up. Finally, demand a timeline: get an arrival estimate and completion window in writing so weather and lead times don’t create surprises.

What causes ice dams on roofs in South Hanover?

Ice dams form when heat escapes into the attic/roof system, melting snow near the top of the roof while colder temperatures refreeze it closer to the eaves. In South Hanover and throughout Pennsylvania, freeze-thaw cycles and frequent winter storms make this problem common—particularly on older homes with less consistent insulation or ventilation. If warm air reaches the roof deck, the snow melt process starts, and once meltwater backs up under shingles, it can enter at the edges where water has the easiest access path.

Common contributors include inadequate attic insulation coverage, air leaks from living spaces into the attic, unbalanced ventilation (not enough intake or exhaust), and missing or insufficient ice and water shield coverage at the eaves. Older installations may have shallow-vented roof lines or details that weren’t designed to manage repeated ice events. Valleys and areas around penetrations are also trouble spots because they concentrate water flow during thaw cycles.

To reduce ice dam risk, ask your roofer to confirm ice and water shield installation at eaves and critical transitions, along with a ventilation plan and insulation recommendations. This is one reason full replacements can cost more than basic repairs in certain homes—yet it’s also a major reason installations last. For isolated damage, roof repair planning often sits around $750–$4,500, but when ice barrier and deck issues are widespread, costs move toward replacement scope ranges.
