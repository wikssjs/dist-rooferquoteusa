Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors are required to register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign, verify the contractor’s HIC number matches the company name on the contract. This matters in Delaware County because reroof scopes often uncover additional work, and you want a contractor operating under the correct licensing framework.

Permits also vary by municipality. Most municipalities in Pennsylvania require a building permit for a full roof replacement (tear-off and new roofing), while repairs typically don’t require the same permit process. Some boroughs may allow like-for-like shingle replacement without a permit, but you should confirm with Yeadon’s building/code office or the responsible Delaware County municipality before work begins.

For protection from winter meltwater, Pennsylvania residential code (based on the IRC) requires ice-barrier protection at the eaves in areas with ice dam history—this effectively applies across Pennsylvania. Ask your roofer to confirm the minimum installation height and where ice/water barrier is being installed (commonly starting at the eaves and extending up-slope).

For storm insurance claims: document photos and damage before cleanup, and request a written scope of loss from the contractor to submit with your insurance claim. PA law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible. Also verify workers’ compensation coverage using WCAIS (PA’s workers’ comp database). As a homeowner, don’t proceed if the crew can’t show proof of coverage—uninsured roofers can create liability exposure for you.

Step-by-step in Yeadon: (1) Check HIC registration at the AG site; (2) Ask for a Certificate of Insurance showing general liability and workers’ comp; (3) Confirm workers’ comp in WCAIS; (4) Pull the municipal permit requirement (full replacement vs repair) with the local code office; (5) Keep all documentation with your contract and inspection records.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Yeadon roof?

With 94.1% of Yeadon’s housing stock built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), many roofs are aging past their intended service life. In Delaware County, the repair-versus-replace decision comes down to the roof’s age, the extent of leaks, and whether the roof deck is still sound. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle (and the resulting ice dam risk on many older rooflines, especially at lower slope-to-eave combinations and improperly insulated homes) can speed up shingle deterioration and water intrusion. That means a roof can “look okay” from the street but still have wet sheathing or compromised underlayment once you open the system.

For older homes around Yeadon, replacement is usually the smarter long-term choice when multiple leak areas appear, when flashing has failed repeatedly around penetrations, or when tear-off reveals decking damage. A targeted repair can be the right call when the leak is isolated and the surrounding system is still in good shape—particularly if your shingles are relatively new and the leak source is clearly accessible (like a failed vent boot, cracked flashing, or a localized storm-damaged section).

Here’s how the pricing logic often plays out. If you’re seeing one active leak and your roof is otherwise solid, a repair may fit into the $300–$1,500 leak-repair planning band—though emergency fixes can sometimes involve additional service calls and short-term protection. But if you’re getting repeated leaks across several sections over a couple of seasons, patchwork commonly costs more than replacement in the $16,500–$32,000 planning range once you factor in labor, rework, and the chance that the underlying problem extends under untouched shingles.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Yeadon

Choosing the right contractor matters in Yeadon because reroofing often uncovers hidden deck damage and requires careful code-aligned installation—especially for winter ice dam protection. Start by verifying certifications and licensing. For PA Home Improvement Contractor registration, look up the company at the Attorney General’s HIC registry (hic.attorneygeneral.gov). If the contractor promotes credentials such as IICRC or equivalent trade certification, ask for the actual certificate and confirm the cert holder is the party performing or supervising the work. For roofing-specific licensing, rely on what Pennsylvania requires for your contractor type and confirm through official registries rather than relying on marketing claims.

Then request 2–3 itemized written quotes. Good quotes break down labor and materials, show removal and disposal details, list underlayment/ice barrier, and identify what’s included around vents, chimneys, skylights, and gutters. Avoid “lump sum only” pricing—roofing scopes should clearly state what is excluded (for example: unseen decking replacement until discovered, code-required ventilation changes, or permit fees). If this is an insurance job, ask whether the contractor will provide documentation for your claim and a written scope of loss that aligns with the actual damage they expect to replace.

Finally, review warranty terms and payment structure. You generally should not pay more than 10–15% upfront, and a holdback until the job is complete is standard. Get an arrival estimate and completion window in writing so you’re not stuck waiting during Delaware County’s busy peak season.

What causes ice dams on roofs in Yeadon?

Ice dams form when heat escapes into the attic and warms the lower portion of the roof deck and eaves, then meltwater refreezes at the colder edge. In Yeadon and much of Pennsylvania, freeze-thaw cycles make this worse—especially on older homes where insulation and ventilation may not match current best practices. You can also see higher risk when roof lines have complex valleys, multiple dormers, or older flashing/edge details that allow water to sit. The presence of 94.1% pre-1980 housing stock (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024) is relevant because many attics in older Delaware County homes weren’t built with modern air-sealing and ventilation strategies. The fix usually isn’t only shingles; it often includes ice and water shield coverage at the eaves, correct installation height as required by PA code practices, plus insulation and ventilation improvements so the roof stays cold and consistent. When you ask for an estimate, confirm the contractor’s plan for eave protection—this is one reason quotes can vary meaningfully across the $300–$1,500 repair band and the full replacement ranges.

What should a roofing quote include in Yeadon?

A strong roofing quote for Yeadon should be detailed enough that you can compare it apples-to-apples. It should list the tear-off scope (including how many layers are being removed, disposal method, and any decking evaluation), the underlayment system, and ice and water shield placement at the eaves. It should specify flashing items (drip edge, step flashing at walls, pipe/vent boots, chimney flashing, and counterflashing where applicable) and describe how they’ll handle gutters and drainage during the reroof process. For flat roofs, the quote should name the membrane type (TPO or EPDM), how penetrations and drains will be flashed, and whether any base repairs are included. The quote should also show whether decking repairs are included or treated as an allowance once exposed. Pricing should align with your project type: for example, asphalt replacements are often planned within the $8,500–$16,500 to higher architectural ranges depending on complexity. How do I know if my Yeadon roof needs replacement?
