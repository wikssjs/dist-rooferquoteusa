Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

For homeowners in Abington, PA, understanding Pennsylvania roofing rules is as important as choosing the material. Roofing contractors are required to register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign anything, verify the contractor’s HIC number matches the business performing the work.

Permits: Pennsylvania municipalities handle roofing permits differently. Many townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not minor repairs). Some boroughs may exempt like-for-like shingle replacement, but the safest approach is to confirm with Abington’s code office or your local municipal authority before work begins. If a contractor tells you “permits aren’t needed,” ask how they verified that for your exact scope and address.

Ice and water shield: Pennsylvania residential code, based on the IRC, requires ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas where ice-dam formation is expected—which applies broadly across the state. Ask your contractor to confirm the required installation method and minimum height (and to show it in the written scope).

Storm insurance claims: document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from the contractor to include with your claim. Pennsylvania law generally prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible; attempting to bypass deductibles can expose you to fraud-related issues.

Workers’ comp: verify coverage using WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database). Uninsured roofers create liability exposure for the homeowner, so confirm proof of workers’ compensation before the first tear-off.

Step-by-step verification for Abington homeowners: (1) check the PA HIC registration online and match it to the contractor’s legal name, (2) request a current certificate of insurance for general liability and workers’ comp, (3) confirm workers’ comp coverage through WCAIS, and (4) ask the contractor to state—on the contract—whether a permit is required and who will pull it.

Bottom line for Abington: if your current roof is aging and you’re noticing recurring moisture (especially after winter melt), replacement can be cheaper long-term than repeatedly fixing the same water path. If the decking is compromised, replacement is the prudent choice to protect your ceilings, insulation, and rafters.
