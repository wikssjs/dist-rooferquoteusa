Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors must be registered as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign, verify the HIC number, because it’s tied to consumer protection requirements. Building permits are handled by local municipalities: most townships and boroughs require a building permit for full roof replacements (not repairs). Some like-for-like shingle swaps may be treated differently, but you should confirm with the Union area code office or your township/borough permitting desk before work starts.

Ice and water shield is a Pennsylvania code expectation based on the IRC framework. In practice, this means protection at the eaves for residential roofs is typically required—especially where ice dams form, which applies broadly across Pennsylvania. Ask your roofer to confirm the minimum required installation height and demonstrate it in the written scope (where it begins, how far up the roofline, and how it’s integrated at valleys and transitions).

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss that you can submit to your carrier. Be cautious: Pennsylvania law and carrier requirements prohibit contractors from “waiving” your deductible—attempts to do so can create insurance fraud exposure. Also confirm workers’ comp coverage through the state’s WCAIS system (PA workers’ comp database) and request a current certificate of insurance showing liability. A properly covered crew protects you from liability if someone is hurt on your property.

Step-by-step for Union homeowners: (1) check the contractor’s HIC at hic.attorneygeneral.gov; (2) request a COI for liability and ensure dates match your project window; (3) confirm workers’ comp via WCAIS and verify the crew/individuals are covered; (4) ask whether a permit is required for a full replacement and who pulls it; (5) get the scope in writing before any tear-off begins.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Union roof?

With 67% of Union’s housing stock built before 1980, many roofs are reaching a point where small leaks turn into larger failures. The right decision in Washington County usually depends on the roof’s age, how widespread the leaks are, and whether the decking is still solid after you remove the damaged materials. In Pennsylvania, freeze-thaw cycles and winter ice dam formation—especially on roofs that don’t have adequate insulation and ventilation—can accelerate shingle deterioration faster than in milder climates.

In Union, you’ll commonly see one of two patterns: either isolated damage (like a few missing shingles after a storm) where repair can make sense, or broader weathering where multiple leak areas appear along valleys, hips, and eaves. A practical rule is that if patchwork would only buy a season or two, replacement often becomes the better value. For example, when a roof is 15–25 years old and has multiple leak areas, the cumulative cost of repeated repairs can climb close to replacement pricing—making replacement the more durable approach within the typical $18,000–$38,000 range.

On roofs 25+ years old, even if the visible leak seems small, the underlying cause is often hidden: compromised underlayment, saturated decking, or flashing that has failed. At that point, replacement is usually the safer route. The same principle applies to flat roofs on porches and additions—repairs can slow symptoms, but membrane bubbling or ponding usually calls for TPO/EPDM replacement to prevent interior damage and repeated emergencies.

Don’t rely on a social media page or a vehicle wrap alone—use the registry.

Next, get 2–3 itemized written quotes. You want line items for tear-off/disposal, underlayment and ice-and-water shield placement, decking repairs, flashing details, ventilation work (if included), and the exact shingle or metal system being installed. Avoid “lump sum” proposals that don’t show what’s excluded, because Union roofs often require deck repair after tear-off.

Read the scope for insurance claim documentation support. A good contractor will offer a written scope of work and photos before cleanup. Ask about warranties: workmanship coverage length and how material warranties are issued (manufacturer warranty vs. installer warranty). Payment schedule matters—never pay more than 10–15% upfront, and hold back a reasonable amount until the job is fully completed and cleaned up.

Finally, timeline should be in writing: an arrival estimate, start date window, and a completion window. On older Washington County homes, availability and material lead times can be 2–6 weeks, so you should plan your schedule early.

Do I need a permit for a roof replacement in Union, PA?

Often, yes. In Pennsylvania, building permits for roofing are determined by your local municipality, and in most townships and boroughs a full roof replacement typically requires a permit rather than a simple repair. That means if you’re doing a full tear-off and replacement of shingles, metal, or a flat roof membrane, you should expect permitting as the norm. For Union homeowners, the safest path is to ask your contractor to confirm whether a permit is required and to identify who will pull it before any tear-off begins. Also remember: even if you’re approved to work, you still need the contractor to be registered as a Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. If you’re unsure, call your Union-area code office with the work description (tear-off, underlayment, flashing, decking, and new material type).

Can I put new shingles over old shingles in Pennsylvania?

Sometimes, but homeowners in Union should assume a tear-off is often required on older roofs. Pennsylvania rules and local enforcement vary, but practically speaking, many Washington County homes were built before 1980 and often already have older layers. When there are multiple shingle layers, adding more shingles usually won’t solve the underlying issues—like saturated underlayment, failing eaves, and ventilation problems that contribute to ice dam risk. Ice dams can loosen shingles and compromise the layers below, so the “over” approach may lock in water damage instead of removing it. If your roof has two existing layers or decking deterioration, a full tear-off with new underlayment and proper ice-and-water shield is typically the durable repair path. Pricing also reflects reality: a standard asphalt replacement in the $9,000–$38,000 band often assumes tear-off and correct underlayment, which is why your quote should explicitly state the layers being removed.

What causes ice dams on roofs in Union?

Ice dams usually come from a combination of warm roof surfaces and cold roof edges. In Union and across Washington County, winter freeze-thaw cycles make this worse when roofs aren’t insulated and ventilated correctly. Common causes include heat loss into the attic, blocked soffit vents, inadequate ridge/roof venting, and air leaks around chimneys or bath fans. When the roof deck stays warm, meltwater runs toward the eaves, refreezes at the colder edge, and forces water under shingles and into joints. That’s why Pennsylvania residential code expectations commonly include ice-and-water barrier protection at eaves—especially in areas where ice dam formation has a history. If your roof is older (and many Union homes are), you may need full replacement of compromised ice-and-water shield and sometimes decking repair. A good leak diagnosis will check the attic air sealing, ventilation balance, and the specific eave/valley locations where ice dams form.

What should a roofing quote include in Union?

A strong Union-area roofing quote should be itemized and transparent—because older homes frequently require “discovered during tear-off” scope changes. The quote should list tear-off details (including how many layers are removed and disposal), underlayment type, and where ice-and-water shield is installed along eaves and other vulnerable transitions. It should specify decking repairs (how damaged OSB/plywood will be replaced), flashing details around chimneys and wall intersections, and any ventilation work included in the scope. For gutters, ask whether replacement of flashing and gutter components is included, since warranty validity often depends on correct integration. Also confirm whether the quote includes cleanup, magnet sweep/vacuuming, and protection of landscaping. Finally, request written warranty information for workmanship and materials. If a contractor’s numbers are only “ballpark,” it’s hard to compare. For example, if you’re budgeting for repair, isolated leak work often lands around $350–$2,500, while full asphalt replacement is commonly estimated within $9,000–$18,000 or higher for larger/steeper roofs.
