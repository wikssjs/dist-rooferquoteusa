Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors are required to register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign anything for a Monroeville roof replacement, verify the contractor’s HIC number matches the company name on the estimate and contract.

Permits vary by municipality. Most townships in the area require a building permit for full roof replacements (not repairs). Some boroughs may exempt certain like-for-like shingle replacements, but you should confirm with the local code office before work starts—especially if you’re changing roof types, adding skylights/vents, replacing decking, or altering flashing and penetration details.

Ice-and-water shield is another regulatory checkpoint. Pennsylvania residential code (based on IRC principles) requires ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas with ice dam risk—which applies broadly across the state. Ask your contractor to confirm the required minimum installation height and how they’ll handle transitions at valleys, dormers, and roof-to-wall intersections.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from the contractor that you can submit with your claim. Also note: Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—attempts to do so can be considered insurance fraud.

Finally, verify workers’ comp coverage using WCAIS (the PA workers’ comp database). In practical terms, you should collect: (1) the HIC verification online, (2) a certificate of insurance naming you/your property as required by the agreement, and (3) proof of workers’ compensation coverage via WC records. A quick checklist like this helps you reduce contractor liability exposure before anyone ladders onto your roof.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Monroeville

Choosing the right contractor is one of the biggest determinants of whether your Monroeville roof lasts its intended lifespan. Start by verifying licensing and certification: Pennsylvania contractors should be listed in the PA Attorney General’s Home Improvement Contractor registry at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. For certification such as IICRC (as applicable), check the cert holder and company listing directly through the cert program’s online directory, then match the name to the contractor you’re considering. Don’t rely on photos—verify credentials before work begins.

Next, request 2–3 itemized written quotes that separate labor and materials (tear-off, disposal, underlayment/ice-and-water shield, flashing, ventilation, and any decking replacement). A lump-sum number without scope details is hard to compare and easier for disputes to arise. Read the scope carefully for what’s excluded: removal of antennas/sat dishes, repair of damaged decking, whether existing gutters are included, and whether permit filing/inspection coordination is handled.

Look closely at warranty coverage. You should receive a workmanship warranty (how long they stand behind installation) and a materials/equipment warranty for shingles, membranes, and key components. Payment schedule should protect you: never pay more than 10–15% upfront, and ask for a holdback until the final inspection and cleanup are complete. Also get an arrival estimate and an expected completion window in writing so you’re not left waiting during Monroeville’s seasonal weather swings.

Can I put new shingles over old shingles in Pennsylvania?

Sometimes, but it depends on your roof’s condition and the number of existing layers. In Monroeville and across Pennsylvania, many older homes built before 1980 have multiple layers already, and stacking new shingles on top can trap moisture and hide decking problems. When we do estimates here, we evaluate whether the roof structure is sound, whether existing shingles are curling, and whether there’s evidence of failed underlayment or ice-dam damage at the eaves/valleys. If your roof has 2+ layers, a proper tear-off is often safer because it allows deck inspection and correction—especially important with Allegheny County’s freeze-thaw cycles and ice dam risk.

From a pricing standpoint, homeowners often compare “save now” versus “fix the cause.” A new architectural asphalt roof commonly sits in the $9,000–$38,000 range installed, but if you need decking repair and ice-and-water shield upgrades after tear-off, the best value usually comes from doing the full correction once rather than repeating patches.

What causes ice dams on roofs in Monroeville?

Ice dams typically form when heat escapes from the home into the attic roof deck, melting snow on the roof. That meltwater runs down until it reaches colder sections near the eaves, refreezes, and creates a dam. In Monroeville, this is common on older housing where insulation/air sealing may not meet current performance standards, and ventilation may be insufficient. The result is water backing up under shingles and underlayment—especially at roof-to-wall intersections, valleys, and dormers.

That’s why Pennsylvania roof practices usually include ice-barrier (ice-and-water shield) protection at eaves and in areas prone to ice dam formation. If your roof shows staining in ceilings near the perimeter or repeated leaks during winter, the root cause is often insulation/ventilation plus roof-edge waterproofing. A reliable repair plan addresses both water entry and the heat/moisture pathway, not just the visible leak spot.

How do I know if my Monroeville roof needs replacement?

Roof age matters a lot. Many asphalt shingle systems are intended for 25–30 years (depending on grade), and older roofs—especially those over 25 years—often have decking and flashing details that don’t hold up through freeze-thaw cycles.
