Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors must be properly registered and able to legally perform Home Improvement work. Before you sign anything, verify the contractor’s Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration through the Pennsylvania Attorney General’s Office site at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Checking the HIC number matters because it’s one of the first filters for whether a roofer is operating within the state’s requirements. For any project that involves structural changes, deck replacement, or changes to the roof system—not just surface repairs—assume you’ll need a permit and plan for review time.

Permits vary by municipality. Most towns and townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not repairs). Some boroughs may handle like-for-like shingle replacement differently, but you should confirm directly with the Scranton-area code office or your specific township/borough before work begins.

Ice-and-water protection is also a regulation topic. Pennsylvania residential code (based on IRC requirements) calls for ice-barrier protection at the eaves in areas with ice dam history, which applies broadly across Pennsylvania. Ask your contractor to confirm the required installation height and where the ice barrier will run—especially on older Scranton roofs.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from your contractor so it can support your claim. Also know that Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—any offer that sounds like “we’ll cover your deductible” should be treated as a serious red flag. Finally, verify workers’ comp coverage via WCAIS so you’re not exposed if a worker is injured on your property.

To verify credentials step-by-step: (1) confirm HIC registration online and match the HIC number to your contract; (2) request a certificate of insurance (general liability) and check the dates and coverage limits; (3) ask for proof of workers’ compensation and verify through WCAIS; and (4) confirm permit responsibility in writing—who pulls the permit, and what inspections are planned.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Scranton roof?

With roughly 87.2% of Scranton-area housing stock built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), many roofs are aging faster than their original lifespans suggest—mostly due to moisture cycles, ice-dam stress, and the way multiple winters can expose small weaknesses. In Lackawanna County, the repair vs. replace decision depends on the age of the current roof, how many areas are leaking, and whether the deck is still solid. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle intensifies this issue, especially on lower edges and roofs affected by ice dams; when meltwater refreezes, shingles can lose adhesion and water can migrate beneath layers.

A useful rule of thumb: if leaks are isolated and the roof system is still relatively young, repair can make financial sense. But when you’re seeing repeated leak zones—around valleys, flashings, or multiple ceiling stains—patchwork becomes a cost treadmill because the underlying system is already compromised. For example, spending on repairs in the first band can sometimes be justified. If you have a roof under 15 years old with one active leak, targeted repair may fall into a range similar to $8,500–$22,000 depending on whether we’re replacing flashing, a section of shingles, or correcting minor ventilation issues.

On the other hand, with a roof 15–25 years old and multiple leak areas, replacement is often the better long-term value—especially because deck work, updated ice-and-water protection, and new ventilation can stop the source rather than chasing symptoms. In cases where the roof is 25+ years old and any damage is found, replacement is usually the safest move because older decking and concealed spaces are much more likely to be compromised. And if you’re dealing with a flat roof membrane bubbling or ponding, repairs are commonly temporary; a membrane replacement is typically what prevents ongoing interior water damage.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Scranton

Start with licensing and credentials. In Pennsylvania, confirm the contractor’s Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration at hic.attorneygeneral.gov, and make sure the HIC number matches the person/business listed on your contract. Ask for proof of workers’ compensation coverage (and verify it through WCAIS if you want a second layer of confidence), and request a current certificate of insurance for general liability before any work starts.

Next, get 2–3 written quotes that are itemized—materials and labor broken out, not just one lump sum. A good quote should list the tear-off scope, underlayment/ice-and-water barrier placement, ventilation plan, flashing and chimney/valley details, and any decking replacement assumptions. If the job is tied to an insurance claim, confirm whether the contractor will provide documentation (photos, a written scope of loss, and measured scope details) so you’re not scrambling later. Also pay attention to what’s excluded: some bids omit gutter replacement, permit fees, or minor deck corrections that turn out to be necessary once shingles come off.

Warranty matters in two parts: workmanship warranty length and what’s covered on materials. Make sure you’re clear on whether warranties require specific installation details (for example, ice-and-water placement and ventilation requirements). For payment, never pay more than 10–15% upfront. Hold back funds until the job is complete and debris is cleared. Finally, schedule timing: request a written arrival estimate and completion window so you can plan around weather and inspections.

Can I put new shingles over old shingles in Pennsylvania?

Sometimes, but not always—and on Scranton roofs with older layers, it’s frequently a poor long-term decision. Pennsylvania rules and municipal permitting can vary, and many contractors will recommend a full tear-off when there are multiple existing layers, suspected deck issues, or signs of ice dam damage. Putting new shingles over old shingles can trap moisture, hide failing flashing, and add weight to the roof structure without correcting the real cause of leaks. For roofs built before 1980 (which make up 87.2% of the area’s housing stock, U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), hidden deterioration is common once the roof is opened—especially around valleys, chimneys, and eaves. If you’re being quoted for a budget reroof in the $8,500–$15,500 range, ask whether it includes tear-off or simply “overlay” installation. A contractor should also explain how they’re addressing ice-and-water barrier requirements at the eaves and whether ventilation will be improved. If the roof deck or flashing needs attention, a full replacement is often the safer choice.

What causes ice dams on roofs in Scranton?

Ice dams in Scranton typically form when heat escapes into the attic space and warms the underside of the roof deck, melting snow. The meltwater then flows to the colder eaves area, refreezes, and creates a blockage that forces water to back up under shingles and into the roof system. Older housing stock is more prone because insulation and ventilation systems often aren’t balanced, and older eave detailing may not provide modern meltwater protection. Pennsylvania also requires ice-barrier protection at the eaves (based on IRC guidance), which is designed to reduce how easily meltwater penetrates—especially in a freeze-thaw climate. If your roof has poor attic airflow, limited soffit intake, or insufficient ridge/roof exhaust, melt patterns get worse. This is why many Scranton homeowners see more edge and flashing-related problems after winter storms. If you’re planning reroofing, ask the contractor how they’ll install ice-and-water shield at the proper locations and what ventilation upgrades they recommend to help prevent repeat ice dam issues.

What should a roofing quote include in Scranton?

A solid Scranton roofing quote should be itemized and match what you’ll actually get installed. At minimum, request a breakdown covering tear-off scope (including how many layers are removed), disposal, underlayment, and ice-and-water barrier placement at the eaves/perimeter. The quote should also list flashing work—chimneys, valleys, wall transitions, and any roof penetrations—plus a ventilation plan (intake and exhaust) tied to your attic configuration. Deck repair assumptions should be included; if rot or damaged sheathing is discovered during tear-off, the contractor should explain how additional deck replacement will be handled with a written change order. Ask whether gutters are included if you need new eavestroughs, since many installers tie gutter replacement to roof warranty validity. For pricing context, a full asphalt shingle replacement might fall around $8,500–$15,500 for simpler scopes, while upgraded architectural or complex roofs can move higher. The quote should also specify warranty terms for workmanship and materials, plus a project timeline and permit responsibility if applicable.

How do I know if my Scranton roof needs replacement?

You usually don’t need to guess—signs of failure and the roof’s age help confirm whether repair will hold or if replacement is the safer route. If you’re seeing multiple leak areas, recurring ceiling staining, widespread granule loss, curling shingles, missing flashing points, or soft/rotted decking, replacement is often the best long-term fix—especially on older roofs. In Scranton’s Lackawanna County housing stock, about 87.2% of homes were built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), so many roofs are operating beyond the life expectancy where minor repairs become frequent. Freeze-thaw cycles can also accelerate deterioration along edges affected by ice dams. A roof that’s 15–25 years old with multiple leak zones is commonly a “patchwork costs more” scenario over the next few winters. Conversely, a roof under 15 years old with a single isolated leak may be repairable.
