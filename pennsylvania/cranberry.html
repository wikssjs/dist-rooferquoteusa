Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors are expected to be properly registered and insured before work begins. One key step: Pennsylvania requires home improvement contractors to be registered through the PA Attorney General’s Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) program. Before you sign anything, verify the contractor’s HIC number at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. This is one of the fastest ways Cranberry homeowners can confirm they’re working with a legitimate business.

Permits vary by municipality. Most townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not repairs), while some boroughs may have different rules for like-for-like shingle replacement. Because Cranberry is served by multiple local code jurisdictions, confirm with your specific borough or township building office what permits are required for your address.

Ice and water shield is another regulatory factor. Pennsylvania residential code (based on IRC requirements) commonly expects ice-barrier protection at the eaves in areas with ice dam history, which applies statewide. Ask your contractor to confirm the required minimum installation height and the details they will follow.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from your contractor for your insurer. Also note: Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—attempts to “make it go away” can become insurance fraud.

To verify workers’ comp coverage, check the WCAIS (Pennsylvania workers’ compensation database). Then, request a certificate of insurance (COI) showing general liability and workers’ compensation. A homeowner-friendly checklist is: (1) confirm HIC registration online, (2) match the company name on the contract to the COI, (3) verify workers’ comp status in WCAIS, and (4) confirm permit requirements with your local building office.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Cranberry

Start by verifying contractor credentials the same way you would in any Pennsylvania community, but with attention to the systems that matter locally. For licensing, confirm the contractor is registered as a Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. For workers’ compensation coverage, ask for proof and verify using WCAIS (Pennsylvania’s workers’ comp database). If a contractor can’t provide documentation promptly, that’s a major red flag—especially for steep-roof safety work.

Next, request 2–3 itemized, written quotes. Avoid lump sums. You want line items that show material quantities, tear-off scope (including how many layers), underlayment and ice barrier, flashing details, ventilation adjustments, and disposal. For insurance claims, make sure the quote includes documentation support: a written scope of loss, a detailed roof assessment, and photo-ready notes your insurer can review.

Warranty matters in Pennsylvania because winter performance depends on correct installation: ask for the workmanship warranty length (not just the product warranty), plus what the manufacturer covers for materials. Payment should protect you: never pay more than about 10–15% upfront, and hold back until the job is complete and cleaned up. Also get arrival estimates and a completion window in writing—spring-to-fall lead times can shift when storm work spikes or specialty crews (metal/flat roof) are scheduled.

What causes ice dams on roofs in Cranberry?

Ice dams form when heat escapes from the home into the attic/roof edge, melting snow near the eaves while colder roof areas refreeze the meltwater. In Cranberry, the freeze-thaw cycle is a big driver—warm/cold swings repeatedly melt and refreeze, stressing underlayment and exposing weak eave details. Older homes are more likely to have ventilation or insulation issues that allow heat transfer, and with about 21.4% of the housing stock built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), that’s a common scenario.

Ice dams can also be worsened by poor air sealing, unbalanced attic ventilation, clogged soffits, or inadequate insulation thickness. Once ice backs up, it can force water under shingles and compromise the ice barrier. That’s why Pennsylvania code expectations (based on the IRC) typically require ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas where ice dams are a known risk. Ask your roofer to explain how they’ll install ice and water shield and confirm the minimum installation height. If you’re seeing ice dam signs, getting roof leak diagnosis early can prevent water damage that otherwise pushes repair costs far beyond isolated patching.
