Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, any contractor performing “home improvement” work should be registered as a Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign anything, verify that the contractor name on the contract matches the HIC registration, and confirm the registration is active.

Building permits for roofing vary by municipality. Most townships require a permit for full roof replacements (not repairs). Some boroughs may exempt like-for-like shingle replacements, but you should confirm with the local code office before work begins—especially when you’re changing roof type, replacing skylights, reworking flashing, or making structural decking repairs.

Ice and water shield: Pennsylvania residential requirements (based on the IRC) call for ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas with a history of ice-dam formation—which applies across virtually all of PA. Ask your contractor to confirm the required installation height and method at eaves, valleys, and other vulnerable areas.

Storm insurance claims require documentation. Before cleanup, take photos and request a written scope of loss from the contractor to support your claim. Pennsylvania law also prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—attempts to do so can be considered insurance fraud.

Finally, confirm workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database). If a contractor can’t prove coverage, the liability risk shifts to the homeowner. In Ellwood City, we recommend homeowners verify: (1) HIC online registry, (2) certificate of insurance for general liability, and (3) proof of workers’ comp coverage through WCAIS.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Ellwood City

Choosing the right roofer in Ellwood City starts with verification. First, confirm the contractor’s HIC registration using the PA Attorney General’s online registry at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Next, ask whether the company carries current general liability and workers’ compensation coverage—then confirm those documents before work begins. Workers’ comp coverage should be verifiable through WCAIS (PA’s database). If a contractor can’t provide proof, do not rely on promises; request documentation in writing.

Then get 2–3 itemized written quotes. Look for a breakdown that shows labor and materials line by line (tear-off, underlayment/ice-and-water shield, ventilation work, flashing details, debris disposal, and any decking repairs). Avoid “lump sum only” bids with vague scopes. Your scope should clearly state what’s included and what’s excluded—such as whether gutter replacement, replacement of damaged flashing, or permit fees are part of the price.

Warranty matters too. Ask for the workmanship warranty length (how long they stand behind installation) and the separate equipment/material warranty terms. Payment schedule should be conservative: never accept more than about 10–15% upfront, and insist on a holdback until the job is complete and cleaned.

Finally, request an arrival estimate and a completion window in writing. Reliable roofers communicate weather-related scheduling and coordinate material lead times.

Can I put new shingles over old shingles in Pennsylvania?

Sometimes, but it depends on what’s already on the roof and how the previous installation was done. In Pennsylvania (including Ellwood City and Lawrence County), many roofs with multiple layers need a full tear-off because decking condition and underlayment age can’t be corrected without removing shingles. If your roof has two existing layers, adding a third can increase weight and can hide problems like brittle shingles, compromised underlayment, or failed ventilation—issues that show up later as leaks during freeze-thaw seasons. With roughly 92.4% of local housing built before 1980, older roof systems are more likely to have wear at edges and valleys where ice-dam risk concentrates. A full replacement typically sits in the asphalt bands that start around $8,000–$17,000 for many replacement scopes (and can be higher with larger steeper roofs or decking repairs). The safest approach is a roof inspection so we can check ventilation, eave conditions, and deck integrity before recommending “overlay” versus tear-off.

What causes ice dams on roofs in Ellwood City?

Ice dams form when heat escapes through the attic and warms the roof surface, causing snow to melt and refreeze near the eaves where temperatures are colder. In Ellwood City, the freeze-thaw pattern combined with older roof insulation/ventilation setups can lead to recurring problems at roof edges and valleys. Poor ventilation (for example, blocked soffit intake or inadequate exhaust) keeps the attic warmer than it should be, and that warming accelerates melt-back even during cold snaps. Another contributor is aging eaves systems—older flashing details and underlayment at the perimeter may be less able to resist moisture intrusion after repeated winters. Pennsylvania code practices—like installing ice-and-water barrier protection at the eaves in areas with ice-dam history—are designed to protect those vulnerable sections (contractors should confirm required installation methods and height). If you’re seeing icicles plus interior staining, don’t just chip the ice; correct the cause with proper underlayment, ice barrier coverage, and balanced attic ventilation during the next roof project.

What should a roofing quote include in Ellwood City?

A good roofing quote in Ellwood City should read like a job description, not just a number. Ask for an itemized scope that covers tear-off (including how many shingle layers will be removed), disposal, underlayment/ice-and-water shield details, ventilation work, flashing replacement at penetrations (chimney/walls/vents/pipe boots), and drip-edge installation. It should also mention decking repair allowances if damage is discovered during tear-off—this is common in older pre-1980 homes where moisture can hide beneath shingles. For flat sections, the quote should specify TPO or EPDM membrane type, detailing at drains/edges/penetrations, and how existing membrane is removed. If gutters are being replaced as part of the warranty package, include that in the scope. A quote also needs a schedule: an expected start/arrival date and an estimated completion window. Finally, it should list warranties clearly (workmanship and manufacturer coverage) and outline what is excluded.

How do I know if my Ellwood City roof needs replacement?
