Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, reputable roofing contractors should already be set up to work legally before they step onto your Washington, PA property. First, Pennsylvania requires roofing contractors to register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before signing anything, confirm the contractor’s HIC number matches the company you’re hiring.

Permits vary by municipality, and in Lehigh County that means your exact rules depend on whether you’re in Washington Township, a borough, or city jurisdiction. Most townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not typical repairs). Some boroughs may exempt certain like-for-like shingle replacements, but you should confirm with the local code office before work begins—don’t rely on verbal assurances.

Ice and water shield matters everywhere in Pennsylvania. Based on residential code (using the IRC framework), eaves need ice-barrier protection where ice-dam history and meltwater intrusion are common—which applies across virtually the entire state. Ask the roofer to confirm the required minimum installation height and where they’ll tie it into flashing details at valleys, penetrations, and transitions.

For storm insurance claims: document damage before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from the contractor to support your insurance submission. Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—it's treated as insurance fraud.

Finally, verify workers’ comp coverage using WCAIS (Pennsylvania workers’ compensation database). Uninsured crews can create liability exposure for homeowners.

How homeowners verify credentials (step-by-step):

  • Check the contractor’s HIC registration on the PA Attorney General site (HIC number + company name match).
  • Request a current certificate of insurance: general liability and workers’ comp.
  • Confirm workers’ comp coverage via WCAIS so you’re not relying on a potentially outdated certificate.
  • Ask what permits are required for your exact address and request the permit number or approval documentation once pulled.
  • Get the full scope in writing: tear-off, underlayment/ice-barrier, flashing, ventilation, and disposal.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Washington roof?

With 53.9% of Washington-area housing built before 1980, many homeowners are dealing with roofs that are at or beyond their expected service life. The repair-versus-replace decision in Lehigh County comes down to three things: the age of the current roof, how widespread the leak pattern is, and whether the decking is still sound after you remove wet or damaged sections. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle makes this more urgent than in milder climates—ice dam formation can lift shingles from the edge and drive moisture under the underlayment, often damaging decking long before homeowners see obvious interior stains.

As a rule, a small isolated leak on a roof with remaining lifespan may justify repair. But if you have multiple leak locations, recurring blistering, or telltale signs that moisture has migrated behind edges, valleys, and penetrations, replacement is usually the smarter long-term choice. For older homes with 54% pre-1980 stock, it’s common to discover that “only a little rot” turns into broader decking replacement once the contractor tears off.

Here’s a practical cost example: if you have multiple leak areas on an asphalt roof that’s 15–25 years old, patchwork can exceed a full replacement over the next few seasons because the labor to isolate and re-seal leaks adds up. In that situation, budgeting within the $24,000–$48,000 replacement range often makes better sense than repeated repairs that land near the low end each time. Flat roof owners with membrane bubbling or ponding also face a similar pattern—repairs tend to be temporary, while membrane replacement better prevents interior drywall damage and recurring wet insulation.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Washington

Choosing the right roofer in Washington means verifying credentials, understanding the real scope, and keeping payment under control. Start with licensing and registration. In Pennsylvania, confirm the Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration through the PA Attorney General’s Office (hic.attorneygeneral.gov). If the contractor is claiming certifications or premium installer status (often tied to shingle or metal brands), ask for documentation showing those credentials and how they are maintained—then check any relevant local registry listings if applicable.

Next, request 2–3 itemized written quotes. “Itemized” matters: you want labor and materials broken out separately, including tear-off scope, underlayment/ice barrier, ventilation upgrades, flashing details, waste/disposal, and how many decking repairs are included or excluded. Make sure the proposal also spells out what is not included—such as hidden rot discovered after removal or interior repair costs.

Read warranty terms carefully. A strong workmanship warranty should cover installation defects for a defined length of time, and separate product warranties should clearly state what materials are covered and what voids coverage (improper ventilation, missing starter strip, poor flashing, or incorrect installation). For payment, never pay more than 10–15% upfront. Hold back final payment until the job is complete, cleaned, and inspected.

Finally, timing: insist on an arrival estimate and completion window in writing. In peak season, reputable contractors may book out several weeks, but you should still receive a schedule you can rely on.

Can I put new shingles over old shingles in Pennsylvania?

Sometimes, but it’s rarely the best move on older Washington roofs. Pennsylvania rules can vary by municipality and by the condition of the existing roof, but the practical issue is condition: if you already have multiple existing layers, aged shingles, or signs of moisture in the deck, an overlay can trap water and shorten the life of the new roof. In Lehigh County, we frequently see 40–70-year-old roofs with at least one prior layer, and homeowners often need full tear-offs because decking and ventilation issues are part of the real problem. Overlay decisions also affect ice-dam risk: if the eave and underlayment details aren’t upgraded correctly, meltwater can still find its way under shingles during freeze-thaw cycles. What causes ice dams on roofs in Washington?

Ice dams happen when heat escapes into the attic, warms the underside of roof sheathing, and causes snow on the roof to melt. That meltwater then refreezes at colder eaves, backing up under shingles and potentially into underlayment. In Washington, the risk is driven by winter freeze-thaw conditions and by many homes having older insulation and ventilation strategies. When attic airflow is poor or insulation coverage is uneven, the roof stays warm enough to create repeated melt cycles, even during cold stretches. Leaky plumbing vents or poorly sealed penetrations can add to the problem, and short overhangs can make cold eave temperatures more aggressive. The most effective prevention is proper attic insulation and ventilation plus correct ice-barrier installation at the eaves and vulnerable roof edges, including valleys and transitions where needed. Pennsylvania residential code (based on the IRC approach) generally expects ice-barrier protection at eaves in areas prone to ice dams. If your contractor only talks about “new shingles” and doesn’t discuss ice-and-water shield height and attic airflow, ask for those details before you commit. For full replacements, that ice-and-water line item can be part of what keeps projects within local bands such as $14,000–$48,000 for architectural shingles.
