Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, you should start every roofing project by confirming the contractor’s registration and your local permit rules. Roofing contractors are required to register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Before you sign anything, verify the HIC number matches the company doing the work.

Building permits for roofing are municipality-specific. In many parts of PA, full roof replacements typically require a building permit, while simple like-for-like shingle replacements can sometimes be exempt in certain boroughs. However, if decking replacement, structural changes, or major reconfigurations are included, permits are much more likely to be required. For Hanover, confirm the permit requirement with your local code office before work starts—especially if the proposal includes decking repairs beyond a light patch.

Ice-and-water shield is a code-driven detail. Based on IRC residential requirements adopted in PA, homeowners in virtually all of Pennsylvania should expect ice-barrier protection at the eaves where ice dams can form. Ask your contractor to state the minimum installation height and show where it’s applied. For storm damage, document everything before cleanup, and request a written scope of loss you can submit with your insurance claim. Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible; that’s considered insurance fraud.

Finally, confirm workers’ comp coverage. Verify through WCAIS (Pennsylvania Workers’ Compensation database) and ask for a current certificate of insurance (and proof of workers’ compensation) before the crew starts.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Hanover

Choosing a roofer in Hanover is about verifying credentials, then reading the paperwork as carefully as the shingles. Start by confirming PA licensing/registration. Roofing contractors must be registered as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Next, ask whether their lead installer holds relevant IICRC certification (where applicable to your roof type) and confirm the certification through the appropriate online registry or issuing organization. Don’t accept screenshots of credentials—ask for the verifiable listing and company name match.

Next, get 2–3 itemized written quotes. Good quotes separate labour and materials (removal, underlayment/ice shield, decking allowance, flashing and ventilation, disposal, and final cleanup) instead of providing only a lump sum. Ask whether the scope includes insurance claim documentation support (photos, measurements, written scope of loss). For warranty, confirm two things: workmanship warranty length and the coverage terms on materials/equipment (especially underlayment, shingles, membrane, and metal components).

Payment schedule matters. Never pay more than about 10–15% upfront, and keep a holdback until the job is complete and you’ve inspected the final roof, vents, and drainage. Finally, require an arrival estimate and completion window in writing so you can plan around prime-season scheduling in the Lehigh Valley (often 3–8 weeks for full replacements).

What causes ice dams on roofs in Hanover?

Ice dams form when heat escapes from the home’s interior through the attic/roof assembly and warms the underside of roof snow. That meltwater flows down until it reaches colder areas near the eaves, where it refreezes into an ice barrier. Over time, water can back up under shingles and enter vulnerable spots like nail lines, valleys, and flashing transitions—especially in older roof systems.

In Hanover and across Pennsylvania, the freeze-thaw cycle is the main climate driver, but the underlying causes are usually roof and insulation/ventilation related: poor attic insulation, air leakage from living spaces into the attic, inadequate intake/exhaust ventilation balance, and insufficient eave protection. Homes built before 1980 are often more likely to need ventilation tuning and updated perimeter detailing due to older construction practices (about 34.3% of homes in the local profile were built before 1980). (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024)
