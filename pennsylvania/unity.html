Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing isn’t just “construction”—there are contractor and permit rules homeowners should verify before signing. First, Pennsylvania requires contractors who do home improvement work to register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office (hic.attorneygeneral.gov). Before work starts, verify the contractor’s HIC number and make sure it matches the contract. This is one of the quickest ways Unity homeowners reduce risk.

Permits for roofing are municipal. Most townships in the area require a building permit for full roof replacements (not just repairs). Some boroughs may exempt like-for-like shingle swaps, but exemptions aren’t universal—confirm with Unity’s applicable municipal code office before the first tear-off. Expect paperwork time and inspection scheduling for full replacements.

On the technical side, ice and water barrier protection is a residential code expectation across Pennsylvania’s climate zones (based on IRC principles), especially along eaves where ice-dam formation is common. Ask your roofer to confirm the minimum installation height and where ice-and-water is applied (typically eaves, valleys, and other at-risk transitions).

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from the contractor so you can submit with your claim. Also note: PA law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—anything suggesting you “don’t need to pay your portion” should be treated as a red flag. Finally, verify workers’ comp coverage via WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database) and ask for proof of insurance; uninsured crews create liability exposure for the homeowner.

Step-by-step for Unity homeowners: (1) check PA AG HIC registration online and record the HIC number; (2) request the certificate of insurance (general liability) naming you as additional insured if applicable; (3) verify workers’ comp in WCAIS; (4) confirm permit needs and inspection schedule with your municipality; (5) keep a copy of the written scope and warranty paperwork in your records.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Unity roof?

With about 60.8% of Unity-area housing stock built before 1980, it’s common to see roofs that are either at the edge of their lifespan or already living with accumulated damage: worn shingles, aging underlayment, and soft spots in plywood discovered during tear-off. The repair-versus-replace decision in Westmoreland County comes down to roof age, how many leak locations you have, and whether decking and flashing details are still sound. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle—especially with ice dam risk on roofs closer to the lower end of typical slopes—can speed up sealant failure and underlayment breakdown. That can turn what seems like a “small leak” into repeated interior staining.

Here’s how it usually shakes out in Unity: If your roof is under 15 years old and the leak is isolated, repairs are often cost-effective, especially when the underlying decking looks stable. But once you’re into the 15–25 year window with multiple leak areas, patchwork can become a recurring expense. For older roofs, replacement is frequently the smarter move because you can remove the built-up layers, inspect the sheathing, and reset the ice-and-water protection where it matters most.

A concrete budget example: two separate leaks that each require local flashing attention might sound cheaper than full replacement, but if you’re already seeing failing underlayment and need repeated sealant and patch repairs over 3–5 years, you may end up paying for multiple service calls. In those cases, a full replacement can be justified even when the starting point feels high—because it consolidates the cost into one project and reduces the odds of “another leak in the same season.” Flat roof sections follow a different rule: membrane bubbling or ponding usually means repairs are temporary, and replacement prevents interior damage and mold risk.

How to choose a roofing contractor in Unity

Choosing the right contractor in Unity comes down to verifying credentials, getting apples-to-apples estimates, and reading the scope carefully. Start with licensing and registration: confirm the roofer’s Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration on the PA Attorney General’s site (hic.attorneygeneral.gov). If you’re considering contractors who advertise additional certifications, verify IICRC (where applicable) through the registry or the certification body’s lookup, and make sure the person performing the work matches the name on your contract.

Next, get 2–3 itemized written quotes (not lump sums). The best bids break out labor, tear-off/disposal, underlayment/ice-and-water, flashing details, ventilation improvements, and any decking replacement. If it’s an insurance job, ask whether the quote includes storm documentation support and a written scope of loss.

Warranty should be clear: separate workmanship warranty length from manufacturer material warranty (and note what voids it). For payment terms, avoid large upfront payments—many reputable roofers keep initial payments around 10–15%. Hold back the remainder until the job is complete, cleaned up, and protected (drip edge, flashing, and gutters installed).

Finally, confirm timeline in writing: expected start date, number of workdays, and an arrival window for the first day. In Westmoreland County, scheduling can be tight during peak season, so a concrete window helps you plan around weather.
