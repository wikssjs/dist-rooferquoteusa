Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing isn’t a “no paperwork” trade—homeowners should verify credentials and permits before work begins. First, roofing contractors must register as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office at hic.attorneygeneral.gov. Confirm the contractor’s HIC number before you sign a contract. If a roofer can’t provide it (or provides a mismatched company name), that’s a red flag.

Permits vary by municipality. In many Allegheny County townships and boroughs, a building permit is required for a full roof replacement (not just a repair). Some places may exempt like-for-like shingle swaps, but because rules are local, verify with Plum Borough or your specific code office before tear-off. Ask what is required for your exact scope: roof replacement, tear-off, deck repair, and any changes to flashing/venting.

Ice and water shield matters statewide: PA residential requirements (based on IRC) typically call for ice-barrier protection at the eaves in areas where ice dams are likely—this applies across Pennsylvania. In practice, you should ask your contractor to confirm the minimum installation height and coverage method for your roofline.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup, and request a written scope of loss to support your claim submission. Be aware: PA law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—it's treated as insurance fraud.

Finally, verify workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS (the PA workers’ comp database) and confirm liability coverage. Step-by-step in Plum: check HIC online, then request a certificate of insurance and proof of workers’ comp coverage, and verify both online.

Next, get 2–3 itemized, written quotes that break down labor and materials. For insurance claims, confirm the scope includes documentation support like photos, measured damage, and a written roof scope you can submit.

Warranty terms also matter. You should receive both a workmanship warranty (how long the install is covered) and a separate materials/equipment warranty (shingles, metal components, underlayment, or membrane). Ask what is required to keep warranties valid, including gutter and flashing installation standards.

For payments, never pay more than 10–15% upfront. Hold back funds until the job is complete, including cleanup and final inspection. Request a written arrival estimate and completion window so weather delays are handled in a documented way.

What causes ice dams on roofs in Plum?

Ice dams form when heat escapes into the attic/upper roof area, melting snow on the roof deck. That meltwater then refreezes at the colder eaves, creating a frozen ridge that blocks proper runoff and can force water under shingles—especially along older rooflines with weak insulation/ventilation. In Plum and across Pennsylvania, freeze-thaw cycles make this problem worse by repeatedly cycling melt and refreeze. Common causes include insufficient attic insulation, ventilation that doesn’t balance intake and exhaust, air leaks around plumbing/chimneys, and roof designs that concentrate cold spots near eaves and valleys. The fix isn’t just scraping ice; it’s addressing the roof system. In many Plum projects, the solution includes proper ice-and-water shield installation at eaves (PA residential code requirements based on the IRC), ensuring the required installation height/coverage, upgrading ventilation, and sealing attic air leaks before insulation upgrades. If you’re budgeting for prevention as part of a replacement, remember that full architectural shingle installs commonly land in the $9,000–$38,000 range depending on your roof complexity.
