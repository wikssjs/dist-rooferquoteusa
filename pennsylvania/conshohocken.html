Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors are expected to be properly registered and insured before they touch your roof. Permits for roofing vary by municipality. Many townships require a building permit for full roof replacements (not just repairs). Some boroughs or specific scenarios may exempt like-for-like shingle replacement, but you should confirm with Conshohocken’s code office or the relevant local permitting department before work begins. Ice and water shield is a recurring requirement across Pennsylvania residential construction (based on IRC). Ask your contractor to confirm the minimum installation height and coverage at eaves and along high-risk lines such as valleys. For storm damage claims, document everything first—photos of interior leaks, exterior damage, and the roof surface—then request a written scope of loss you can submit with your insurance paperwork. Pennsylvania law generally prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible (considered insurance fraud), so avoid any offer that asks you to “ignore” that portion.

Finally, verify workers’ compensation. Use WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database) to confirm coverage, and request a certificate of insurance for general liability. Step-by-step, a homeowner should: (1) confirm the contractor’s HIC number online, (2) check WCAIS for workers’ comp, (3) review the COI certificate, (4) confirm permit requirements with the local code office, and (5) ensure the written contract matches the scope used for any insurance claim.

Can I put new shingles over old shingles in Pennsylvania?

What causes ice dams on roofs in Conshohocken?

Ice dams happen when heat escapes into the attic and warms the underside of snow on your roof, then refreezes at the cold eaves. In Conshohocken and across Pennsylvania, freeze-thaw cycles amplify the problem—especially when insulation is inadequate, attic ventilation is unbalanced, or older homes have eave details that don’t include the right protective layers. When meltwater can’t run off properly, it backs up under shingles and can lead to leaks into soffits and ceilings.

Common contributors we see on older Montgomery County homes include poor roof ventilation, air leakage from living spaces into attics, and incomplete or failing ice-and-water shield at the eaves and along vulnerable lines. That’s why Pennsylvania residential code guidance (based on IRC principles) expects ice-barrier protection for many installations, particularly where ice-dam history is common. A contractor should be able to confirm the required minimum installation height and show how valleys and roof-to-wall transitions are treated.

If you suspect an ice dam, don’t just clear snow repeatedly—address the cause with insulation and ventilation fixes, and ensure the underlayment system is correct. Repairing a single leak might be in the neighborhood of $400–$3,500 if truly isolated, but recurring ice-dam damage often leads to higher replacement scopes as materials degrade underneath.
