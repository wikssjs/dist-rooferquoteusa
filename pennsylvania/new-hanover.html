Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, roofing contractors are required to be registered as Home Improvement Contractors (HIC) through the PA Attorney General’s Office. Before you sign anything, verify that registration at hic.attorneygeneral.gov and confirm the HIC number matches the company name on your contract and invoices.

Permits also matter. In many Montgomery County municipalities, a building permit is typically required for full roof replacements (especially when you’re doing a complete tear-off, changing materials, or replacing decking). Some like-for-like shingle swaps may be treated differently, but you should confirm with your local code office before work starts. Plan on permit timing and scheduling—especially if your property has restrictions around work hours or dumpster placement.

For winter performance, ice and water shield protection is a standard residential requirement under Pennsylvania code (based on the IRC) where ice-dam risk is present—which applies broadly across the state. Ask your roofer to confirm the required minimum installation height at the eaves for your home’s roof layout.

For storm insurance claims, document everything before cleanup. Request a written scope of loss from the contractor so it can support your insurance submission. Also note: Pennsylvania law prohibits contractors from waiving your deductible—this can be considered insurance fraud. For coverage, verify workers’ compensation via WCAIS (Pennsylvania’s Workers’ Compensation insurance database) and request proof of WC coverage and general liability.

Step-by-step for New Hanover: (1) confirm HIC registration online, (2) request your roofer’s certificate of insurance showing your project address is covered, (3) verify workers’ comp coverage via WCAIS, and (4) ensure the permit path is handled—either by the contractor or in coordination with you—before the crew begins tear-off.

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your New Hanover roof?

With 32.2% of New Hanover’s housing stock built before 1980, it’s common to encounter roofs that are already past their planned service life or that have underlying issues masked by “surface” shingle damage. The repair vs. replace decision in Montgomery County hinges on roof age, where leaks are happening, whether the decking is still solid, and whether ventilation and underlayment meet today’s ice-dam risk expectations.

Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle accelerates deterioration when water intrudes at the eaves and valleys—particularly on roofs with lower slopes and on areas where insulation and air sealing are lacking. If the leak pattern suggests recurring ice damming, replacing only shingles can fail again during the next cold snap because the underlayment and wood structure may already be compromised.

But if you’re seeing multiple leak areas, recurring flashing failures, or soft spots that show up during inspection, patchwork can cost more over 3–5 years. For many homeowners, a full architectural asphalt replacement commonly lands in the $9,500–$24,000 range for straightforward homes, but more complex tear-offs, decking repairs, and ventilation upgrades can move the total toward $24,000–$45,000.

In New Hanover specifically, those older pre-1980 roofs often require decking and underlayment upgrades to truly “fix the cause,” not just the symptom—so replacement can be justified even when the shingles look only partially damaged from the ground.

How to choose a roofing contractor in New Hanover

Picking the right contractor in New Hanover starts with verification. First, confirm the contractor’s Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration number via the PA Attorney General’s website, then check whether they hold the proper licensing and discipline status required for their work. While requirements can vary by scope, you should always ask for proof of licensing/registrations that apply to the company you’re hiring, and keep copies for your records. For workers’ comp coverage, verify through WCAIS and request a certificate of insurance for general liability and workers’ compensation.

Next, get 2–3 itemized written quotes (not a one-line lump sum). A good quote breaks out labor and materials: tear-off disposal, underlayment/ice-and-water shield coverage, decking replacement, flashing details (chimneys/step flashing/valleys), ventilation work, and gutter replacement if it’s included. Make sure the scope clearly states what’s excluded, such as hidden water damage remediation or drywall repair inside your home.

Warranty should be more than a tagline: ask for workmanship warranty length and whether manufacturer warranties for shingles or metal panels transfer to you. Clarify material warranties and coverage conditions (for example, fastening method and starter strip requirements).

Payment scheduling should protect you: never pay more than about 10–15% upfront. Hold back the remainder until the job is complete, inspected, and any punch-list items are finished. Finally, require arrival and completion timing in writing—peak-season scheduling in the Montgomery County area can run 3–8 weeks, so having dates reduces surprises.

How do I know if my New Hanover roof needs replacement?

You typically need roof replacement in New Hanover when the roof’s age and damage pattern suggest recurring failure, not just a single spot issue. Decking condition matters too: if an inspection shows soft spots, water staining that extends beyond the shingle layer, or rot along eaves and valleys, repairs tend to be temporary. Pennsylvania’s winter freeze-thaw cycles also mean a roof can “look okay” from the street while still suffering ice-dam water intrusion under the shingles. When homeowners built before 1980 (which applies to a significant share of the area) start getting winter leaks, the underlayment and ice protection may need full renewal as part of a proper replacement. As a planning reference, some localized issues may fall under the “leak / isolated damage” approach, but once a full tear-off and re-sheeting is needed, replacement budgets often align with the $9,500–$24,000 to $24,000–$45,000 bands depending on complexity.
