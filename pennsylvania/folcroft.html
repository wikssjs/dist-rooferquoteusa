Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

Building permits for roofing are municipality-specific. In many PA municipalities, a permit is required for full roof replacements (not repairs), particularly when tear-off is involved. Ask your contractor to confirm the minimum installation height and how they’ll install it at valleys, low-slope transitions, and perimeter areas.

For storm insurance claims, document first—photos, dates, and an organized scope of loss from the contractor to submit with your claim. If the crew isn’t covered, you can face liability exposure. Step-by-step for Folcroft homeowners: (1) check HIC at hic.attorneygeneral.gov, (2) request a certificate of insurance showing general liability and workers’ comp, (3) confirm workers’ comp with WCAIS, and (4) ensure your contract includes permit handling (or written confirmation that no permit is required for your specific scope).

Repair vs. replace — what makes sense for your Folcroft roof?

With about 91.9% of Folcroft’s housing stock built before 1980, many roofs are aging alongside the home’s original ventilation, flashing, and insulation strategy. That’s why the repair-versus-replace decision in Delaware County depends less on “one missing shingle” and more on the roof’s overall condition after inspection and tear-off. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycle increases ice dam risk—especially on roof lines that are susceptible to ice accumulation—so damage can start as minor leaks and then spread to decking, underlayment, and interior finishes.

In practical Folcroft terms, we typically look at roof age, number of leak locations, visible wear (cupping, cracking, failed seal tabs), and—most importantly—decking integrity. If there are multiple leak points, widespread granule loss, or suspected underlayment failure, replacement can be the smarter long-term move because you eliminate the hidden water pathways and reset the underlayment system.

For instance, several leak repairs may add up to a portion of the $16,500–$32,000 replacement band, while replacement can address decking, flashing, and ventilation corrections in one coordinated scope (instead of repeating those issues every winter).

What causes ice dams on roofs in Folcroft?

Ice dams form when heat escapes into the attic/roof space and melts snow on the upper roof, then the melted water refreezes near the colder eaves and gutters. Over time, repeated freeze-thaw cycles can push water under shingles and into flashing areas. In Folcroft and the rest of Pennsylvania, ice dam risk is heightened by older roofing assemblies where insulation and ventilation may not meet today’s performance standards, and where ice-barrier protection may be incomplete or worn out. Older pre-1980 homes are more likely to have ventilation patterns and insulation levels that allow roof heat to escape, turning snowmelt into trapped runoff.

Ice dams are also worsened by roof geometry—valleys, dormers, and areas with poor airflow can stay colder and create “freeze points.” If you’ve got a leak before winter, don’t assume it’s only a shingle issue; it may be underlayment or ice-and-water barrier failure at the perimeter. In many cases, the right fix includes emergency leak stopping, then scheduled improvements like proper ice-and-water installation at eaves, and ventilation/insulation upgrades to reduce future melt/refreeze cycles.
